## 1. Historical Significance and Value Foundations  

The value of old Singer sewing machines stems from their revolutionary impact on textile production and domestic labor. Isaac Merritt Singer’s 1851 patent for the presser-foot mechanism transformed sewing from a manual craft into an industrialized process[5]. By standardizing stitch quality and speed, Singer machines became indispensable in households and factories alike.  

The Singer Manufacturing Company’s global expansion—including its South Bend, Indiana, cabinet factory that employed 3,000 workers by 1914—cemented its role in American industrial history[5]. Machines produced during this era often feature ornate black walnut cabinets, reflecting the company’s investment in aesthetic design to appeal to middle-class consumers.  

**Case Study**: The 1928 Singer Model 66-1, identified by serial number AC360494, exemplifies this blend of form and function. With its oscillating hook and low-shank design, it remains sought after for its mechanical reliability and Art Deco-era decals[6].
