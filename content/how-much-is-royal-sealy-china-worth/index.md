## 2. Historical Development and Cultural Context  

### Post-WWII Production and Export  
Following Japan’s surrender in 1945, the Allied occupation government mandated that exports bear the “Made in Occupied Japan” mark to regulate trade[13]. Royal Sealy emerged during this period, leveraging Japan’s ceramic expertise to produce affordable bone china for Western consumers. The brand’s designs often incorporated Western floral and fruit motifs, catering to American and European tastes while utilizing traditional Japanese techniques like *noborigama* kiln firing[15].  

### Evolution of Design  
Early pieces (1940s–1950s) favored minimalist shapes with hand-painted details, while 1950s–1960s designs embraced bolder lusterware and intricate reticulated saucers.
