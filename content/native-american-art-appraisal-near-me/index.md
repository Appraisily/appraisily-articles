## Historical Background and Development of Native American Art Appraisal  

The appraisal of Native American art emerged alongside the late 19th-century fascination with Indigenous cultures, driven by collectors and anthropologists seeking to preserve artifacts. Early appraisals were often informal, conducted by traders or scholars with limited understanding of cultural context. By the mid-20th century, institutions like the Denver Art Museum began collaborating with specialized appraisers to catalog collections, blending anthropological insight with market analysis[1][14]. The 1970s marked a turning point, as federal laws such as the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) emphasized the ethical stewardship of cultural items, necessitating appraisals that balanced monetary value with tribal significance[14][16].  

In the 21st century, accreditation bodies like the International Society of Appraisers (ISA) standardized practices, requiring adherence to Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP)[7]. Appraisers such as Jack Lima and Aarin Richard have refined methodologies, incorporating provenance research, material analysis, and consultations with tribal experts[2][7]. For example, McAllister/Fossum Appraisal Services in Seattle combines art historical training with Indigenous knowledge to evaluate Northwest Coast carvings, ensuring both market accuracy and cultural respect[8].

## Factors Determining Value and Quality  

The value of Native American art hinges on **authenticity**, **provenance**, **craftsmanship**, and **cultural significance**.  

1. **Authenticity**: Materials and techniques must align with historical practices. For example, pre-1950s Navajo textiles using vegetal dyes command higher prices than modern reproductions[5][16].  
2. **Provenance**: Documentation tracing ownership to renowned collectors or exhibitions enhances value. A Plains beaded jacket once owned by a 19th-century tribal leader might appraise for 30–50% more than similar items without provenance[12].  
3. **Condition**: Damage from pests, light, or improper storage can reduce value by up to 70%. Conservators at the Kluge-Ruhe Collection prioritize stabilizing flaking pigments on bark paintings to prevent further deterioration[15][16].  
4. **Cultural Context**: Ceremonial items like Kwakwaka’wakw masks require appraisers to balance market demand with ethical considerations, often restricting commercial sale to respect tribal protocols[8][14].

## Preservation and Care Strategies  

Proper care extends an artwork’s lifespan and value:  

1. **Environment**: Maintain 40–50% humidity and 65–70°F to prevent material degradation[16].  
2. **Handling**: Use gloves for textiles and pottery to avoid oil transfer[14][16].  
3. **Storage**: Store baskets upright on padded shelves; hang textiles flat to prevent stress[15][16].
