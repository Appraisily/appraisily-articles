## 1. Definition and Importance of Antique Appraisals  
An **antique appraisal** is a certified evaluation of an item’s market value by a credentialed expert, typically for:  
- **Insurance coverage** (ensuring adequate protection against loss/damage)  
- **Estate settlements** (fair asset distribution)  
- **Resale/auction preparation** (pricing strategy)  
- **Tax documentation** (charitable donations, estate taxes)  

Local appraisals offer **in-person inspection advantages**, such as assessing subtle details like tool marks, repairs, or provenance documentation that photos might miss[1][8]. For example, Prestige Estate Services provides three appraisal formats: in-person, remote, and photo-based, with fees ranging from $150–$500+ depending on complexity[1][6].

## 2. Historical Development  
- **Ancient Origins**: Early appraisal practices emerged in **Egypt** (3000 BCE) to authenticate jewelry for trade and social status[2].  
- **19th-Century Formalization**: The rise of antique collecting led to appraisals focusing on **historical/artistic merit**, not just material value[2].  
- **Modern Professionalization**: Organizations like the **International Society of Appraisers** (founded 1979) established standardized methodologies and ethics[12].

## 4. Value Determinants  
Key factors appraisers analyze:  
1. **Rarity**: Limited-production items (e.g., 1920s Art Deco jewelry) command premiums[4].  
2. **Provenance**: Ownership by historical figures can increase value by 30–50%[4][13].  
3. **Condition**: Pristine 18th-century furniture may appraise 2–3× higher than restored pieces[8].  
4. **Market Trends**: Post-2020, mid-century modern design surged 40%, while Confederate memorabilia declined[13].

## 5. Identifying Qualified Appraisers  
- **Credentials**: Seek **ISA** or **AAA** certification[7][12].  
- **Specialization**: Verify expertise (e.g., Asian ceramics vs. vintage watches)[5][12].  
- **Conflict-Free**: Avoid appraisers who offer to buy items they evaluate[14].  

**Red Flag Example**: An appraiser claiming a 1930s vase is "priceless" without providing comparable sales data[5].
