# The Comprehensive Guide to Old Ford Tools Identification  

## Key Findings Summary  
The identification of old Ford tools is a specialized field crucial for automotive historians, restoration enthusiasts, and collectors. These tools, produced between the early 1900s and the mid-20th century, reflect Ford Motor Company’s engineering innovations and manufacturing practices. Authentic tools are distinguished by part numbers, Ford script markings, and design features tied to specific vehicle models like the Model T (1908–1927) and Model A (1927–1931). Their value stems from historical significance, rarity, and role in preserving automotive heritage. This guide synthesizes historical context, identification methodologies, market dynamics, and preservation strategies, drawing on primary sources from Ford archives, collector forums, and restoration manuals[1][2][6][18].  

---

## Historical Background and Development  

### Early Ford Tool Kits (1908–1930s)  
Ford’s tool production evolved alongside its vehicles. The **Model T** (1908–1927) introduced standardized toolkits to empower owners with basic maintenance capabilities. These kits included adjustable wrenches, pliers, tire irons, and jacks, often marked with Ford script or part numbers[2][5]. By the **Model A** era (1927–1931), toolkits expanded to 13 items, such as spark plug wrenches and grease guns, with subtle design changes reflecting manufacturing advancements[1][14].  

#### Key Milestones:  
- **Pre-1914**: Early tools lacked part numbers and Ford branding, complicating identification. Model T pliers, for example, initially omitted screwdriver tips[6].  
- **1928–1931**: Model A tools introduced part numbers (e.g., A-17036B for cranks) and standardized finishes, though many remained unmarked[1][9].  
- **Post-1932**: Tools began featuring alphanumeric codes (e.g., 01A-17017) indicating production dates and applications, a system still used today[8][18].  

### Manufacturing Partnerships  
Ford collaborated with toolmakers like **Moore Drop Forging** and **Herbrand** to produce specialized service tools. The **5-Z series** (e.g., 5-Z-1901 for cylinder head nuts) were dealer-exclusive tools marked with Ford script and manufacturer logos[18][19]. These partnerships ensured quality but led to variations in markings, complicating modern identification[6][8].

## Types and Categories of Old Ford Tools  

### 1. **Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) Tools**  
- **Model T Tools**: Early examples include lobster-claw pliers (T-1903 A1) and spark plug wrenches (T-5893)[5][6].  
- **Model A Tools**: Featured in Restoration Guidelines & Judging Standards, including tire pumps (A-17050) and adjustable wrenches with 3/8" square ends[1][14].  
- **V8 Era (1932–1953)**: Tools like the M-01A-17017B combo wrench adopted stamped part numbers and “Ford USA” branding[9][18].  

### 2. **Service and Specialty Tools**  
- **5-Z Series**: Factory tools for dealers, such as the 5-Z-1705 rear hub puller for Model AA trucks[18].  
- **KR Wilson Tools**: Third-party tools licensed by Ford, including valve grinders and piston ring squeezers[18].  

### 3. **Aftermarket and Reproduction Tools**  
Post-1950s reproductions often lack authentic markings. For example, unmarked pliers or adjustable wrenches with incorrect finishes are common[6][16].

## Factors Determining Value and Quality  

### 1. **Authenticity**  
- **Ford Script**: Genuine tools feature raised or stamped Ford logos. Post-1932 tools often include “USA”[4][8].  
- **Part Numbers**: Early tools (pre-1932) rarely had numbers, while later ones use codes like 01A-17017 (January 1941 production)[8][9].  

### 2. **Rarity**  
- **Model T Oil Cans (Pre-1928)**: Discontinued after 1928, surviving examples fetch $200+[1][16].  
- **Canadian-Market Tools**: Scarce due to limited production, commanding premiums[16][18].  

### 3. **Condition**  
- **Unrestored vs. Restored**: Light surface rust may retain value, while over-polishing reduces authenticity. Electrolysis cleaning preserves patina[12][19].  
- **Completeness**: A 1928 Model A toolkit with original jack and grease gun sold for $1,200, while incomplete sets struggle to reach $500[16].

## Identifying Authentic Tools  

### 1. **Markings and Codes**  
- **Ford Script**: Compare to known examples (e.g., Model A wrenches vs. V8-era “Ford USA”)[6][8].  
- **Part Numbers**: Decode using Ford’s system:  
  - **01A-17017**: 01 = January, A = 1941, 17017 = tool category[8][9].  
  - **5-Z-1901**: 5-Z denotes service tools, 1901 = cylinder head wrench[18].  

### 2. **Design Features**  
- **Model T Pliers**: Early versions lack screwdriver tips; post-1914 models include them[6].  
- **Adjustable Wrenches**: Model A tools have a 3/8" square end for rear axle plugs[1][4].  

### 3. **Material Analysis**  
- **Chrome Vanadium Steel**: Post-1930s tools use this alloy, while earlier tools are high-carbon steel[4][12].

- **Model A Jack Handle**: $80–$150[16].

### 3. **Reproductions**  
- **Modern Copies**: Sold by suppliers like Snyder’s, often 30–50% cheaper than originals[14][16].

## Sourcing and Selling Old Ford Tools  

### 1. **Acquisition Channels**  
- **Swap Meets and Auctions**: Model A National Meets feature dedicated tool sections[16][17].  
- **Online Platforms**: eBay listings (e.g., 1931 toolkit listed at $450) and forums like Ford Barn[16][17].  

### 2. **Authentication Services**  
- **NAFTCO**: The National Ford Tool Collectors organization certifies tools and publishes resource guides[17].  

### 3. **Selling Strategies**  
- **Provenance Documentation**: Tools with ownership histories fetch 20–30% premiums[16][17].  
- **Bundling**: Pair common tools (screwdrivers) with rare items (grease guns) to boost lot value[16].

## Preservation and Maintenance  

### 1. **Cleaning Techniques**  
- **Electrolysis**: Removes rust without damaging original finishes[12][19].  
- **Chemical Treatments**: Muriatic acid soaks (outdoors only) restore heavily corroded tools[19].  

### 2. **Storage Recommendations**  
- **Climate Control**: Humidity below 50% prevents rust[12][19].  
- **Original Packaging**: Tool bags or period-correct boxes enhance display value[1][14].  

### 3. **Restoration Ethics**  
- **Patina Preservation**: Clear coats (e.g., Rust-Oleum) protect surfaces without altering appearance[12][19].  
- **Avoid Over-Restoration**: Judicious use of wire wheels retains historical integrity[12][19].
