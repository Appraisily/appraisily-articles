# The Comprehensive Guide to Old Coca-Cola Bottles: Value, History, and Collectibility  

Old Coca-Cola bottles represent a fascinating intersection of industrial design, marketing history, and American nostalgia. From the iconic contour shape to rare Hutchinson-era specimens, these glass artifacts have become prized collectibles. This guide synthesizes historical records, auction data, and preservation techniques to provide an authoritative resource for collectors and researchers.  

---

## Historical Evolution of Coca-Cola Bottle Design  

### Early Bottling Innovations (1894–1915)  
The first Coca-Cola bottles emerged in 1894 as Hutchinson-style containers with wire-and-rubber stoppers. These amber or clear glass bottles bore embossed text like "Biedenharn Candy Company, Vicksburg, Miss." and are now valued at $2,000–$4,000 in pristine condition[6]. The 1915 Root Glass Company prototype—a precursor to the contour bottle—redefined branding with its distinctive fluted design. Only a handful survive, with one selling for $240,000 in 2023[5].  

### The Contour Revolution (1916–Present)  
Adopted nationwide by 1920, the contour bottle became synonymous with Coca-Cola. Early variants featured:  
- **Embossed patent dates**: "Dec 25, 1923" bottles (called "Christmas Cokes") now fetch $15–$200 depending on bottling location[8]  
- **Date codes**: Post-1938 bottles display numeric stamps (e.g., "42 L 87" = 1942 production)[2]  
- **Manufacturer marks**: Symbols like "C in a circle" (Chattanooga Glass) or "O-I" (Owens-Illinois)[3]

## Authentication and Identification Techniques  

### Decoding Manufacturer Marks  
- **NBBG Co**: North Baltimore Bottle Glass (1888–1926)[3]  
- **7**: Owens-Illinois plant code for Alton, IL[3]  
- **D-105529**: Patent denoting 1938–1951 production[2]  

### Date Verification Methods  
1. **Embossed Codes**:  
   - Pre-1965: No painted labels; date stamps near waist (e.g., "50" = 1950)[3]  
   - Post-1965: Paper labels with printed dates  

2. **Shape Analysis**:  
   - Hutchinson (pre-1915): 26 oz capacity, wire stopper  
   - Straight-sided (1916–1951): Cylindrical body, "PATENT D" base[6]
