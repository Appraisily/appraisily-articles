## Historical Context and Significance of Bicentennial Quarters  

### Origins of the Bicentennial Coin Program  
The United States Bicentennial coinage emerged from congressional legislation passed in 1973, mandating temporary redesigns of the quarter, half-dollar, and dollar[4]. Unlike the 1876 Centennial, which relied on medals, the 1976 program aimed to integrate commemorative designs into circulating currency. A nationwide design competition yielded three iconic reverses: Jack L. Ahr’s colonial drummer for the quarter, Seth Huntington’s Independence Hall for the half-dollar, and Dennis R. Williams’ Liberty Bell superimposed on the Moon for the dollar[4][5].  

Production began in 1975 to meet anticipated demand, with all coins bearing the dual date "1776–1976." Over 1.6 billion quarters were struck across three mints: Philadelphia (no mint mark), Denver ("D"), and San Francisco ("S")[1][4]. The San Francisco Mint also produced 4 million 40% silver proof quarters and 5 million silver uncirculated specimens for collectors[1][7].

## Classification and Varieties  

### Compositional Differences  
1. **Clad Quarters (Circulation Strikes)**:  
   - Composed of outer layers of 75% copper/25% nickel bonded to a pure copper core.  
   - Philadelphia and Denver produced 809 million and 860 million clad quarters, respectively[4][6].  
   - Circulated examples typically retain face value, while uncirculated (MS63–MS67) grades range from $3 to $50[2][6].  

2. **40% Silver Proofs and Uncirculated Coins**:  
   - Struck exclusively at San Francisco, these contain 40% silver and 60% copper.  
   - Proofs (PR69–PR70) fetch $15–$288, while uncirculated silver quarters (MS69) have sold for $5,060–$19,200[1][8].  

### Mint-Specific Variations  
- **1976-P (Philadelphia)**: No mint mark. High mintage ensures abundance, though MS65+ examples are scarce[6].  
- **1976-D (Denver)**: Identified by the "D" mint mark. Notable for "filled D" errors, where excess metal obscures the mark, elevating values to $20–$50[3][5].  
- **1976-S (San Francisco)**: Silver proofs and uncirculated coins. A rare "no S" silver proof, struck by error, sold for $5.1 million in 2025[8].

## Determinants of Value  

### Grading and Condition  
The Sheldon Scale (1–70) dictates premiums:  
- **Circulated (G-4 to AU-58)**: Face value to $1.  
- **Uncirculated (MS63–MS67)**: $3–$50.  
- **Gem Grades (MS68–MS70)**: $100–$19,200[1][6].  

### Mint Errors and Rarity  
- **Double Die Obverse (DDO)**: Doubled lettering on "LIBERTY" or Washington’s profile. Examples fetch $500–$8,400[3][11].  
- **Off-Center Strikes**: Misaligned strikes retaining full dates. A 40% off-center 1976-D quarter sold for $6,463[8].  
- **Silver Planchet Errors**: Clad quarters struck on 40% silver blanks. Only three confirmed examples exist, valued over $75 million[11].
