# Comprehensive Analysis of the 1962-D Lincoln Penny: Value, History, and Market Trends  

The 1962-D Lincoln penny, a staple of American numismatics, holds both historical significance and potential collector value. Minted in Denver during a transitional period in U.S. coinage, its worth ranges from face value to over $1,000 for pristine or error specimens. This report synthesizes historical context, production details, grading criteria, and market dynamics to provide a definitive guide for collectors and researchers.

## Historical Background and Development  

The 1962-D Lincoln penny emerged during a pivotal era in U.S. coinage. Introduced in 1909 to commemorate Abraham Lincoln’s centennial, the Lincoln cent series initially featured wheat ears on its reverse. In 1959, the design shifted to the Lincoln Memorial to mark the 150th anniversary of Lincoln’s birth, a motif retained through 2008[3][9]. The 1962-D penny, struck at the Denver Mint, reflects this design lineage, with Victor D. Brenner’s obverse portrait of Lincoln and Frank Gasparro’s Memorial reverse[1][4].  

A critical historical nuance lies in its composition. Pennies minted in 1962 consisted of 95% copper and 5% zinc, a formula unchanged since 1909. However, Congress authorized the removal of tin in late 1962, transitioning to a brass alloy (95% copper, 5% zinc) to simplify production[3][10]. This makes the 1962-D penny one of the last issues with trace tin content, though its primary value derives from numismatic factors rather than metallurgy[1][9].  

Denver produced 1,793,148,400 examples, dwarfing Philadelphia’s 606 million[1][9]. Despite this high mintage, well-preserved specimens remain sought after, particularly in higher grades[2][4].

## Types, Categories, and Variations  

### Standard Issues  
- **Business Strikes**: Circulated 1962-D pennies typically trade for $0.01–$0.25, while uncirculated examples (MS60–MS64) range from $1–$20[1][2][10].  
- **Mint State (MS) Grades**: Coins graded MS65 (gem quality) command $20–$100, with MS67 specimens reaching $1,000–$1,800 at auction[2][3][10].  

### Proofs and Special Strikes  
Though Philadelphia minted 3.2 million proof pennies in 1962, Denver did not produce proofs. Philadelphia proofs in cameo or deep cameo finishes fetch $30–$35 in PR67[3][9].  

### Notable Errors and Varieties  
- **Full Brockage Reverse**: A 1962-D penny with a mirror-image obverse struck on the reverse sold for $500 in AU53[3][7].  
- **Struck Fragments**: An irregularly shaped fragment graded MS64 realized $1,000[3][7].  
- **Repunched Mintmarks (RPM)**: Minor doubling on the “D” mintmark adds a 10–20% premium[2][6].

## Factors Determining Value  

### 1. **Grading and Condition**  
The Sheldon Scale (1–70) dictates value. Red (RD) coins retain full original luster, while red-brown (RB) or brown (BN) specimens see diminished premiums[1][3]. For example:  
- MS63 RD: $3–$7[3][10]  
- MS67 RD: $1,000–$1,800[2][10]  

### 2. **Color Preservation**  
“Red” designation requires 85–95% original copper luster. A 1962-D graded MS67+ RD sold for $12,000 in 2013, underscoring the premium for color retention[10].  

### 3. **Mint Errors**  
Errors like off-center strikes or die cracks amplify value. A 1962-D struck 30% off-center sold for $150[6][11].  

### 4. **Market Demand**  
Registry set collectors and error specialists drive competition. PCGS reports only 15 MS67+ examples, creating scarcity[9][10].

## Identification and Authentication  

### Key Features  
- **Obverse**: Lincoln’s portrait with “IN GOD WE TRUST” and the date. The “D” mintmark appears below the date[1][4].  
- **Reverse**: Lincoln Memorial with “UNITED STATES OF AMERICA” and “ONE CENT”[1][6].  

### Detection of Alterations  
- **Weight and Dimensions**: Authentic pennies weigh 3.11g with a 19mm diameter[4][10]. Deviations suggest counterfeits or post-mint damage.  
- **Magnetic Testing**: Genuine 1962-D pennies are non-magnetic (copper content)[10].  

### Professional Grading  
Services like PCGS or NGC authenticate and encapsulate coins. A 1962-D graded MS67 RD by PCGS sold for $1,560 in 2024[9][10].
