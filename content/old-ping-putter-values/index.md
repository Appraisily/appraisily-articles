# Comprehensive Analysis of Old Ping Putter Values  

## Key Findings Summary  
Old Ping putters represent a unique intersection of golf history, engineering innovation, and collectible value. From Karsten Solheim’s garage-crafted designs in the 1950s to modern limited-edition releases, these putters have shaped golf equipment evolution. Values range from $100 for common used models to over $1,000 for rare Redwood City–stamped classics, driven by factors like historical significance, materials, and provenance. The market thrives on platforms like Next Round Golf and 2nd Swing, while preservation techniques such as vinegar-based cleaning and protective waxing maintain their condition.

## Historical Background and Development  

### Origins of Ping Putters  
The story begins in 1959 when Karsten Solheim, an engineer frustrated with his putting, crafted the first Ping putter in his garage. The distinctive “ping” sound at impact became synonymous with the brand[4]. His **Ping 1A**, featuring heel-toe weighting, revolutionized putter design by improving forgiveness and accuracy[10]. This innovation laid the groundwork for the **Anser**, patented in 1966 as an “answer” to competitors’ designs[4]. The Anser’s blade-style head and thin face became a template for modern putters, with over 500 tournament wins attributed to it, including George Archer’s 1969 Masters victory[4][10].  

### Evolution Through Decades  
- **1960s–1970s**: Early models like the **Scottsdale** and **Redwood City**–stamped putters (e.g., Anser 2) emerged, with manganese bronze construction[1][3]. These are now highly collectible, with Redwood City models valued over $1,000[1].  
- **1980s–1990s**: Introduction of beryllium copper in models like the **Zing 2**, prized for its soft feel[3][10]. The **Karsten Series** further refined weight distribution[10].  
- **2000s–Present**: Modern lines like the **Sigma 2** and **PLD Milled** series incorporate adjustable shafts and CNC-milled faces[7][14]. Limited editions, such as the 2016 50th Anniversary Anser, replicate original molds and sell for premium prices[4][14].

## Types and Variations  

### By Era and Material  
1. **Vintage Models (Pre-1980)**  
   - **Redwood City Stamps**: Produced in Ping’s original California facility, these include the Anser 1A and Scottsdale models. Values exceed $1,000 due to rarity[1][3].  
   - **Manganese Bronze**: Used in early Ansers, this alloy develops a patina over time, which collectors either preserve or polish[12][15].  

2. **Classic Models (1980s–2000s)**  
   - **Beryllium Copper**: The **Zing 2** (1982) and **Karsten B60** (1978) offer a softer feel, with prices ranging from $120–$300[10][13].  
   - **Stainless Steel**: The **Anser 2** used by Tiger Woods in the 1990s remains a benchmark, selling for $150–$200[4][13].  

3. **Modern Collectibles (Post-2010)**  
   - **Limited Editions**: The **PLD Milled Anser 30** (2025) retails for $1,800+, while the 2016 50th Anniversary Anser sells for $4,990 in collector sets[14].  
   - **Tour-Proven Models**: Seve Ballesteros’ **Pal 2** and Mark O’Meara’s **Anser** are sought after, with prices reflecting their major championship pedigrees[4][14].

## Factors Determining Value  

### Rarity and Provenance  
- **Production Location**: Putters stamped “Redwood City” (pre-1966) or “Scottsdale” (1960s) command premiums[1][3].  
- **Limited Runs**: Only 88 units of the 1988 “Ping Slam” set (Pal, Zing2, Anser, Pal2) were made, now valued at $4,990[14].  
- **Celebrity Ownership**: Tiger Woods’ early Anser 2 putters fetch up to $5,000 at auction[4].  

### Condition and Authenticity  
- **Originality**: Unaltered grips, shafts, and finishes increase value. A 1968 “Old Blade” with its original grip sold for $450[5][15].  
- **Restoration Risks**: Over-polishing can erase historical patina, reducing value by 30–50%[12].  

### Material and Craftsmanship  
- **Beryllium Copper vs. Stainless Steel**: The former’s rarity and feel make it 20–40% more valuable than stainless counterparts[10][13].  
- **CNC Milling**: Modern PLD models, like the **Anser 4D**, use aerospace-grade aluminum for precision, justifying their $1,800+ price[7][14].

## Identifying Authentic Models  

### Key Markers  
1. **Stamps and Engravings**  
   - Pre-1980 models feature “Redwood City” or “Scottsdale” stamps[1][3].  
   - Post-1980 putters include ZIP codes (e.g., “85020” for Phoenix)[15].  

2. **Weight and Balance**  
   - Vintage Ansers weigh 320–330 grams, while modern counterparts exceed 350 grams[10].  
   - Counterfeit models often have uneven weight distribution or incorrect face milling[11].  

3. **Serial Numbers**  
   - Post-1990 models include laser-etched serials, verifiable through Ping’s customer service[6][8].  

### Case Study: Spotting Fakes  
A 2015 Golf Monthly forum post highlighted a counterfeit **Anser 3** with a misspelled “PHEONIX” stamp and unclear ZIP code. Authentic models use “PHOENIX” and precise 5-digit codes[11].

## Preservation and Maintenance  

### Cleaning Techniques  
1. **Vinegar-Salt Soak**: Removes tarnish without damaging bronze (10-minute soak, followed by Brasso polish)[12].  
2. **Protective Waxing**: Meguiar’s car wax prevents re-oxidation after cleaning[12].  

### Storage Guidelines  
- **Climate Control**: Store in low humidity to prevent patina acceleration.  
- **Headcovers**: Use original or silicone-treated covers to avoid finish scratches[12][15].  

### Restoration Ethics
