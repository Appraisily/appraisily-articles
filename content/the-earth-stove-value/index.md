## 1. Defining the Earth Stove and Its Historical Significance  

### 1.1 Conceptual Foundations  
The term “earth stove” refers to a class of heating and cooking devices constructed primarily from earthen materials such as clay, adobe, or compressed soil. Unlike metal stoves, earth stoves leverage the thermal retention properties of their constituent materials to radiate heat gradually, making them energy-efficient solutions for domestic and communal use[1]. Their design often integrates functional and aesthetic elements, with surface carvings or glazes reflecting regional artistic traditions.  

### 1.2 Historical Evolution  
Earth stoves trace their origins to Neolithic settlements, where early humans transitioned from open fires to enclosed combustion chambers. In China, the *kang*—a heated bed-stove system—emerged during the Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE), utilizing flues to distribute heat from a central firepit[2]. Similarly, the Roman *hypocaust* and Korean *ondol* systems exemplify the global prevalence of earth-based heating technologies. These innovations not only improved living conditions but also influenced architectural layouts, as homes were designed around centralized stove structures.
