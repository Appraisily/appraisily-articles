Martin saxophones, particularly vintage models, are highly sought after by collectors and musicians for their craftsmanship, tonal quality, and historical significance. Below is a comprehensive guide to understanding their value and context.

---

## 1. **What Is Martin Saxophone Value?**  
Martin saxophone value refers to the market worth of instruments produced by the Martin Band Instrument Company, particularly vintage professional models like the **Committee series**. These saxophones are prized for:  
- **Sound quality**: Known for a "big, vibrant" tone with excellent intonation[5][14].  
- **Craftsmanship**: Features like soldered tone holes, intricate engravings, and durable mechanics[1][8].  
- **Historical relevance**: Used by jazz legends like Art Pepper and Louis Jordan[10][14].  
- **Collectibility**: Rare models (e.g., Committee III, Magna) appreciate due to limited supply and growing demand[3][8].  

---

## 2. **Historical Background**  
- **Founding**: Established in 1865 by John Henry Martin, a German immigrant[7][11].  
- **Key Models**:  
  - **Handcraft Committee (1930s)**: Nicknamed "Skyline" for art-deco engravings[9][10].  
  - **Committee II (1939)**: Larger bell, "Lion and Crown" engraving[9].  
  - **Committee III (1945–1971)**: Renamed "The Martin," favored for R&B and rock[9][13].  
- **Ownership Changes**: Acquired by Wurlitzer (1964) and Leblanc (1971), with production shifting to Japan[7][11].

## 4. **Factors Determining Value**  
- **Condition**: Original lacquer (80–90% increases value)[5][14], minimal dents[1].  
- **Authenticity**: Serial numbers (e.g., 166996 = 1948 alto)[5], original neck/engraving[13].  
- **Rarity**: Low-A baritones, curved sopranos, and gold-plated models command premiums[4][8].  
- **Playability**: Functional microtuners, rolled tone holes, and intact pads[1][16].  
- **Provenance**: Ownership by notable musicians or limited editions (e.g., Centennial)[8][9].  

---

## 5. **Identifying Authentic Models**  
- **Serial Numbers**: Cross-reference with charts (e.g., 150,XXX = 1944 alto)[13].  
- **Engravings**: Look for "The Martin" on the bell and tapered tone holes[5][13].  
- **Mechanical Features**: Button-lock necks (pro models only)[5], Norton springs[4].  
- **Case Studies**: A 1944 Committee III alto with 85% lacquer sold for $2,500[13], while relacquered models lose 10–20% value[2].  

---

## 6. **Market Trends and Pricing**  
- **Entry-Level**: Student models (Indiana, Medalist) fetch $300–$800[6][8].  
- **Mid-Range**: Committee I/II altos average $1,800–$2,900[4][10].  
- **High-End**: Committee III tenors and Magna baritones reach $7,500+[2][4].  
- **Demand**: Rising interest in pre-1960s "vintage tone" horns, with prices increasing 5–10% annually[3][8].

## 7. **Buying/Selling Channels**  
- **Specialized Dealers**: Sax Gourmet, Junkdude, and KGUmusic offer restored models[4][14].  
- **Auctions**: eBay and Reverb list rare models (e.g., Typewriter baritones)[4].  
- **Appraisals**: Services like Carter Vintage assess authenticity via UV light and serial checks[15].  

---

## 8. **Maintenance and Preservation**  
- **Daily Care**: Swab moisture, dry pads with cigarette paper, and polish with chamois[16][18].  
- **Storage**: Use humidity-controlled cases and avoid temperature swings[16].  
- **Professional Servicing**: COA (cleaning, oiling, adjustment) every 18 months[16][17].  
- **Avoid Corrosion**: Dry soldered tone holes thoroughly to prevent galvanic damage[19].
