## Historical Context and Economic Significance  

### Origins During Monetary Transition  
The 1935 $1 Silver Certificate emerged during the Great Depression as part of Franklin D. Roosevelt’s New Deal reforms[7]. Unlike earlier "Funny Back" designs from 1928–1934, this series introduced a compact format with symbolic imagery:  
- **Obverse**: George Washington portrait with blue Treasury seal  
- **Reverse**: Pyramid/All-Seeing Eye motif and Great Seal of the United States[1][5]  

These certificates fulfilled dual purposes:  
1. **Economic Stabilization**: Backed by Treasury silver reserves, they restored public confidence during bank failures[4][7]  
2. **Metal Conservation**: Reduced reliance on scarce silver coinage by providing paper alternatives[3][6]  

The redemption privilege for silver bullion or coins persisted until 1968, when Congress terminated the practice amid rising metal prices[4][6].

### Critical Design Variations  
1. **Motto vs. No Motto**

   - **1935 (With Motto)**: Added in 1957 redesign; <0.3% survival rate[5]  

2. **Mule Notes**  
   Hybrids pairing 1935A fronts with 1934-era backs exhibit mismatched plate numbers (0.5mm vs. 1mm)[1][6]. These error notes fetch $150–$500 in circulated grades[1].

## Authentication Protocols  

### Genuine 1935 $1 Markers  
1. **Blue Elements**  
   - Treasury seal and serial numbers (non-reflective ink)[4][5]  
2. **Fibrous Paper**  
   - Red/blue security threads under UV light[4]  
3. **Microprinting**  
   - "United States" repeated along borders (magnification required)[4]  

Counterfeit red flags include blurred details, incorrect paper texture, and missing plate numbers[4].
