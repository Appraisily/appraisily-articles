# The Comprehensive Guide to the Value of 1975 Roosevelt Dimes  

The 1975 Roosevelt dime, a staple of American coinage, holds a fascinating position in numismatics due to its potential for extraordinary value under specific conditions. While most 1975 dimes remain common pocket change, certain rare varieties—particularly the **1975 No S Proof dime**—have achieved legendary status, with one example selling for **$506,250** in 2024[4][6][8]. This report explores the historical context, key variations, valuation factors, and market dynamics that define the 1975 dime’s worth, offering collectors and researchers a detailed roadmap to understanding this iconic coin.

## Historical Context and Design Evolution  

### Origins of the Roosevelt Dime  
Introduced in 1946 to honor President Franklin D. Roosevelt’s legacy and his advocacy for the March of Dimes, the Roosevelt dime replaced the Mercury dime. Designed by Chief Engraver John R. Sinnock, the coin faced initial resistance from the Commission of Fine Arts, which critiqued its aesthetic simplicity[2][3]. The obverse features Roosevelt’s left-facing profile, accompanied by the inscriptions “LIBERTY,” “IN GOD WE TRUST,” and the year. The reverse depicts a torch flanked by olive and oak branches, symbolizing liberty, peace, and strength[2][3].  

### Compositional Shift in 1965  
Originally struck in 90% silver, the dime’s composition changed in 1965 due to rising silver prices. The new clad structure—a copper core layered with 75% copper and 25% nickel—reduced production costs and eliminated hoarding incentives[2][3]. This composition remains standard for circulation strikes, including the 1975 dimes.

## Key Varieties and Rarity Factors  

### Mint Marks and Production Figures  
In 1975, dimes were produced at three U.S. Mint facilities:  
1. **Philadelphia (No mint mark):** 585.7 million struck[2][3].  
2. **Denver (D mint mark):** 313.9 million struck[2][3].  
3. **San Francisco (S mint mark):** 2.8 million proof coins struck for collectors[2][3].  

While Philadelphia and Denver dimes are common, San Francisco’s proof issues—intended for collector sets—hold higher intrinsic value due to their mirror-like finishes and precise strikes[2][3].  

### The 1975 No S Proof Dime: A Numismatic Anomaly  
The **1975 No S Proof dime** is one of the rarest U.S. coins, with only two confirmed examples[4][6][8]. This error occurred when a proof die lacking the “S” mint mark was mistakenly used at the San Francisco Mint. Both known specimens were discovered in original proof sets: one in 1977 and another in 1984[6][11]. In 2024, the latter sold for **$506,250**, nearly 30 times its 1978 purchase price[6][8].  

### Full Band Designation  
Coins designated **Full Band (FB)** exhibit sharply defined horizontal bands on the reverse torch. For example, a 1975 Philadelphia dime graded MS67-FB sold for **$2,250**, compared to **$200** for a non-FB example[2][3]. This attribute significantly elevates value, particularly in higher grades.

## Factors Influencing Value  

### 1. Mint Mark and Rarity  
- **No mint mark (Philadelphia):** Common in circulated grades; values rise sharply in MS67+ (e.g., **$200–$2,250**)[2][3].  
- **D mint mark (Denver):** Similar to Philadelphia issues but rarer in high grades (e.g., **$550** for MS67-FB)[2][3].  
- **S mint mark (Proof):** Standard proofs fetch **$4–$110**, while the No S error commands **$500,000+**[2][4][6].  

### 2. Condition and Grading  
The Sheldon Scale (1–70) determines a coin’s marketability:  
- **Circulated (Grades 1–58):** Worth face value to **$1**[2][3].  
- **Mint State (MS60–70):** MS67 examples range from **$40** (Denver) to **$2,250** (Philadelphia FB)[2][3].  
- **Proof (PR60–70):** Deep Cameo proofs reach **$110**, while the No S error tops **$500,000**[2][6][8].  

### 3. Errors and Varieties  
- **Repunched Mint Marks:** 1975-S/S dimes (double-struck “S”) sell for **$800+** in PR69[3][9].  
- **Off-Center Strikes:** Misaligned dies can increase value by **50–100%**, depending on severity[3][9].

## Authentication and Identification  

### Detecting the 1975 No S Proof Dime  
1. **Proof Characteristics:** Mirror-like fields and frosted devices distinguish proofs from circulation strikes[3][11].  
2. **Mint Mark Absence:** The obverse should lack an “S” above the date[1][6].  
3. **Professional Grading:** Services like PCGS and NGC authenticate and encapsulate coins, verifying legitimacy[6][9].  

### Tools for Collectors  
- **Magnification:** A 10x loupe reveals repunched mint marks or subtle doubling[3][9].  
- **Reference Guides:** PCGS and NGC population reports track graded examples, aiding rarity assessments[6][9].

## Preservation and Care Techniques  

### Storage Best Practices  
- **Climate Control:** Store in a stable environment (50–70°F, 40–50% humidity) to prevent oxidation[10][12].  
- **Holders:** Use inert materials like Mylar flips or acrylic slabs to avoid chemical reactions[10][12].  

### Handling Protocols
