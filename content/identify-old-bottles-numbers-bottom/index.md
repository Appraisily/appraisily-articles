## 1. Purpose and Value of Bottom Numbers  
Numbers embossed on bottle bases primarily served practical manufacturing purposes:  
- **Mold identification**: Single/double-digit numbers (e.g., "12") identified specific molds in multi-cavity production systems, enabling quality control tracking[1][10].  
- **Date coding**: Post-1920s bottles often include two-digit year indicators (e.g., "64" for 1964)[1][3].  
- **Factory codes**: Numbers paired with logos indicated production plants (e.g., "20" for Owens-Illinois’ Oakland, CA facility)[1][8].  

For collectors and historians, these numbers help:  
- Authenticate pre-1950s bottles (90% of modern bottles lack meaningful markings)[14]  
- Trace regional manufacturing trends (e.g., "D-1" codes for National Distillers’ liquor bottles)[1]  
- Establish provenance for rare specimens (a 1932 Owens-Illinois flask recently sold for $1,200 at auction)[7]

## 5. Authentication Techniques  
**Step 1: Examine manufacturing traits**  
- **Seam lines**: Machine-made bottles (post-1905) have vertical seams extending to the lip[14]  
- **Pontil scars**: Rough, circular marks indicate pre-1860 hand-blown production[9]  

**Step 2: Decode markings**  
- Cross-reference numbers with manufacturer archives (e.g., Dominion Glass mold lists)[10]  
- Verify date code consistency (e.g., "64" shouldn’t appear on pre-1964 designs)[1]  

**Step 3: Material analysis**  
- Pre-1917 glass often contains bubbles and impurities[11]  
- UV light detects modern reproductions (post-1950s glass fluoresces differently)[15]
