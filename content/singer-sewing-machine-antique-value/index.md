## Historical Background and Development  

### Origins of Singer’s Sewing Machine Legacy  
The Singer Manufacturing Company, founded by Isaac Merritt Singer in 1851, revolutionized textile production through patented innovations such as the rigid-arm frame and continuous-feed mechanism[2]. Early models like the Singer Model 1 (1851) and Model 2 (1852) introduced treadle-powered operation, replacing hand-cranked designs. By 1885, Singer pioneered electric sewing machines, with mass production commencing in 1910[11]. The company’s global expansion—including the Kilbowie Factory in Scotland (1884), which became the world’s largest manufacturing plant—solidified its dominance[11].  

### Evolution of Key Models  
- **Model 15 (1895–1960s):** The first machine with a vertical oscillating shuttle, enabling smoother stitching. Over 10 million units were produced, making it one of the most ubiquitous antique models[11].  
- **Featherweight 221 (1933–1964):** A 11-pound portable machine with aluminum casing, celebrated for its precision in quilting. Over 1.4 million units sold, with rare variants like the 1939 crinkle-finish model fetching $5,400 at auction[8].  
- **Treadle Machines (Pre-1920):** Cast-iron bases with ornate decals, often housed in oak cabinets. A fully restored 1897 treadle machine with brass accents recently appraised for $1,800[7][12].  

Singer’s marketing innovations, including installment payment plans (1856) and free sewing courses (1863), democratized access to sewing technology while embedding the brand into domestic life[11].
