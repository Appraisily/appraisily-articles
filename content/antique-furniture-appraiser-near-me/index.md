## 1. Defining Antique Furniture Appraisal and Its Importance  

An **antique furniture appraiser** is a certified professional who evaluates the age, condition, provenance, and market value of furniture items typically over 100 years old. Their assessments adhere to the **Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP)**, ensuring consistency and reliability[1][3]. Appraisals are vital for:  

- **Insurance Coverage**: Determining replacement value for policies[3][10].  
- **Estate Settlements**: Equitable asset division during probate or divorce[2][9].  
- **Market Transactions**: Setting fair prices for buyers and sellers[8][12].  
- **Historical Preservation**: Documenting craftsmanship and provenance for cultural institutions[13][16].  

For example, a 19th-century rosewood secretary appraised by Prestige Estate Services was found to have originated from a renowned Parisian workshop, increasing its insured value by 300% due to its provenance[6]. Without such expertise, owners risk undervaluing heirlooms or overpaying for inauthentic pieces.

## 2. Historical Evolution of Antique Furniture Appraisal  

The practice emerged alongside the antiques trade in the late 19th century, as industrialization spurred nostalgia for handcrafted furniture. Early appraisers were often dealers or collectors, relying on anecdotal knowledge. Professionalization began in the 1960s with organizations like the **International Society of Appraisers (ISA)** and the **Appraisers Association of America (AAA)**, which established credentialing systems[5][6].  

The Antique Trader in Denver, operational since 1965, exemplifies this shift from informal valuation to standardized practice. Their adoption of USPAP in the 1980s aligned with broader industry trends toward forensic analysis, including dendrochronology (wood dating) and ultraviolet light inspections to detect repairs[1][11]. Today, digital tools like online appraisal platforms (e.g., Mearto) and auction databases (e.g., Sotheby’s Price Database) have further refined accuracy[11][12].

### Environmental Controls  
- **Humidity**: Maintain 35–50% to prevent warping; a 2024 study found 60% humidity caused $12M in damage to New England collections[15][16].
