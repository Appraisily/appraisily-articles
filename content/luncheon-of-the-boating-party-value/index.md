## 2. Historical Development of the Painting’s Value  

### 2.1 Creation and Early Reception  
Painted in 1881, *Luncheon of the Boating Party* was conceived during Renoir’s frequent visits to Maison Fournaise, a restaurant-hotel on the Seine River[6][14]. The work took 16 months to complete, interrupted briefly when Renoir broke his right arm in a cycling accident, forcing him to paint with his left hand[15][16]. Despite this challenge, the painting debuted to acclaim at the 1882 Impressionist Exhibition, with critic Paul de Charry noting its “fresh and free” vitality[11]. Art dealer Paul Durand-Ruel purchased it immediately, recognizing its commercial and artistic potential[1][8].  

### 2.2 Acquisition by Duncan Phillips  
In 1923, American collector Duncan Phillips acquired the painting from Durand-Ruel’s estate, spending his entire annual art budget to secure it[8][16]. Phillips viewed the work as “one of the greatest paintings in the world,” a centerpiece for his nascent Phillips Memorial Gallery (now The Phillips Collection)[4][5]. His acquisition marked a turning point in the painting’s valuation, transitioning it from a European masterpiece to an American cultural icon[4][8].  

### 2.3 Modern Appraisal and Legacy  
Today, the painting’s value is bolstered by its status as the Phillips Collection’s most visited artwork[2][4].
