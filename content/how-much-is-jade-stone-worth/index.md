# The Comprehensive Guide to Jade Stone Valuation: Factors, Trends, and Historical Significance  

## Introduction to Jade's Value and Cultural Importance  
Jade has captivated human civilization for millennia, serving as both a artistic medium and symbolic artifact across Asian cultures. Valued higher than gold in ancient China[9][10], this gemstone's worth stems from its unique combination of physical durability, aesthetic appeal, and deep cultural symbolism. The most expensive jadeite necklace ever sold—the Barbara Hutton-Mdivani piece containing 27 imperial green beads—achieved $27.44 million at auction[4], demonstrating jade's status as one of Earth's most precious materials. Contemporary prices range from $5 per carat for low-quality nephrite to $27.4 million per carat for record-setting specimens[3], with market dynamics shaped by geological rarity, craftsmanship, and shifting consumer preferences in luxury markets[7].

## Historical Evolution of Jade Valuation  

### Neolithic Beginnings to Imperial China  
Jade's story begins in China's Hongshan (4700-2200 BCE) and Liangzhu (3400-2250 BCE) cultures, where artisans crafted ritual objects like *bi* discs and *cong* tubes from nephrite[11][14]. These early works established jade as a medium connecting earthly and spiritual realms. By the Zhou Dynasty (1046-256 BCE), Confucian philosophy codified jade's symbolic virtues—benevolence, wisdom, courage—directly linking material value to moral philosophy[12][15].  

The Han Dynasty (206 BCE-220 CE) marked a valuation milestone with jade burial suits, where nobles were interred in garments made from thousands of nephrite plaques sewn with gold thread[10][12]. A single suit for Prince Liu Sheng contained 2,498 jade pieces[10], illustrating early price inflation driven by spiritual beliefs about immortality.  

### Jadeite's Market Disruption  
Burmese jadeite's 18th-century introduction revolutionized valuations[9]. Unlike nephrite's muted tones, jadeite's vivid greens—particularly "Imperial Jade"—commanded unprecedented prices. The 1997 "Doubly Fortunate" necklace sale at $9.39 million[4] exemplified this shift, with jadeite dominating high-end markets through intense color saturation (Cr-green specimens achieving 630-690 nm absorption spectra)[4].
