## Historical Evolution of Troll Dolls  

### Origins in Scandinavian Folklore  
The modern troll doll traces its roots to Scandinavian mythology, where trolls were depicted as supernatural beings inhabiting remote forests and mountains[5]. Danish woodcutter Thomas Dam transformed these legends into tangible form in 1959, crafting the first commercial troll dolls from rubber and wood after losing his job post-World War II[4][5]. Dam’s initial creations, hand-carved for his children, gained local popularity in Gjøl, Denmark, leading to the establishment of Dam Things A/S in 1959[5]. These early dolls featured soft Icelandic sheep wool hair and glass eyes, distinguishing them from later mass-produced imitations[4].  

### Commercial Expansion and Copyright Challenges  
By 1962, Dam’s factory transitioned to PVC molding to meet international demand, with the Uneeda Doll Company distributing them in the U.S. as “Wishnik” dolls[1][5]. However, flawed U.S. copyright filings allowed competitors like Norfin and Ideal Toy Company to flood markets with knockoffs by 1963[1][5]. This legal oversight inadvertently propelled troll dolls into the public domain, triggering a 1960s fad that saw $100,000 in monthly U.S. sales[1]. Despite Dam securing copyrights in 1965, the proliferation of imitations created a fragmented market that persists in collector circles today[5].  

### Resurgences and Modern Iterations  
Troll dolls experienced renewed popularity waves in the 1980s (Norfin era), 1990s (neon-haired variants), and 2010s (DreamWorks’ *Trolls* franchise)[3][5]. The 2020s have seen premium pricing for 1950s–1960s Dam originals, exemplified by a 3-inch 1960s Dam monkey troll selling for $875 in 2024[1]. Meanwhile, contemporary manufacturers like Nyform continue producing artisanal latex trolls priced between $31–$120, bridging vintage appeal with modern craftsmanship[6].

## Determinants of Market Value

Early Dam dolls used durable vinyl and natural fibers, whereas 1960s knockoffs relied on brittle plastics. A 1959 rubber Dam doll with wool hair recently sold for $1,025, while a 1964 plastic imitation fetched just $45[4][11].  

### Condition Grading  
- **Mint (90–100% original):** $300–$1,200 for 1960s Dam dolls[3][11].

## Authentication and Appraisal Techniques  

### Hallmarks of Authenticity  
1. **Foot Markings:** Genuine Dam dolls bear “Dam” or “Dam Denmark” stamps[4].  
2. **Hair Texture:** Icelandic wool feels coarse compared to synthetic knockoff fibers[4].  
3. **Eye Material:** Pre-1970 Dam dolls used glass eyes; later models transitioned to plastic[10].  

### Counterfeit Detection  
- **Inconsistent Proportions:** Knockoffs often have oversized heads or misaligned limbs.  
- **Anachronistic Materials:** PVC bodies in dolls marketed as 1950s-era are likely replicas.

## Preservation and Restoration  

### Cleaning Protocols  
1. **Surface Dirt:** Use diluted Woolite and soft brushes, avoiding eye areas[8][9].  
2. **Mildew Removal:** Submerge in 10% benzoyl peroxide gel, then sun-bleach for 48 hours[10].  
3. **Odor Elimination:** Febreeze sprays followed by 72-hour airing[10].  

### Structural Repairs  
- **Rewigging:** Heat vinyl to 180°F (82°C) for 10 minutes to soften glue, then insert mohair[10].  
- **Cracked Eyes:** Replace with 8mm acrylic eyes ($12/pair via Troll Hole Museum)[10].  

### Storage Recommendations
