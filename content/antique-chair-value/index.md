## Determinants of Value and Quality  

### Material Authenticity  
Pre-20th-century chairs predominantly use **solid woods** like mahogany or oak, with hand-cut dovetail joints indicating pre-industrial craftsmanship[5][9]. Veneers, if present, should be thick and uneven, unlike machine-pressed modern versions[5].  

### Construction Techniques  
Hand-forged nails, irregular screw threads, and tool marks distinguish antiques from reproductions. A 17th-century Windsor chair’s spindle back, for instance, shows subtle asymmetries absent in factory-made copies[2][9].  

### Condition and Restoration  
Minimal intervention preserves value: A Queen Anne chair with original finish may appraise 40% higher than a refinished counterpart[1]. However, structural repairs using period-appropriate methods, like hide glue for joint reinforcement, maintain integrity without penalizing value[8].  

### Provenance Verification
