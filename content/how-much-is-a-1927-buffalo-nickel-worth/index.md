# The Comprehensive Guide to the 1927 Buffalo Nickel: Value, History, and Market Insights  

The 1927 Buffalo Nickel, a quintessential piece of early 20th-century American coinage, holds significant numismatic value due to its historical legacy, artistic design, and rarity in higher grades. Minted in Philadelphia, Denver, and San Francisco, its worth ranges from under $1 for heavily circulated examples to over $100,000 for pristine or error-containing specimens. Key factors influencing value include mint mark, condition (graded from "Good" to "Uncirculated"), and the presence of rare errors like the "3½ Legs" or "Two Feathers" varieties. This report synthesizes historical context, grading criteria, market trends, and preservation strategies to provide collectors and researchers with a definitive resource on this iconic coin.

## 1. Historical Background and Development of the 1927 Buffalo Nickel  

### Origins of the Buffalo Nickel Series  
The Buffalo Nickel, designed by sculptor James Earle Fraser, debuted in 1913 under President Theodore Roosevelt’s initiative to beautify U.S. coinage[2][6]. Fraser’s design featured a composite portrait of three Native American chiefs (Iron Tail, Two Moons, and Big Tree) on the obverse and an American bison, Black Diamond, on the reverse[2][5]. The series faced immediate challenges: the Hobbs Manufacturing Company protested the initial design (Type 1) for its incompatibility with vending machines, leading to a modified "recessed ground" reverse (Type 2) later in 1913[2][5].  

### Production Challenges and 1927 Context  
By 1927, the Buffalo Nickel’s production was well-established but plagued by die wear and weak strikes due to high-volume minting[5]. Three mints produced the 1927 issue:  
- **Philadelphia**: 37,981,000 coins (no mint mark)  
- **Denver (D)**: 5,730,000 coins  
- **San Francisco (S)**: 3,430,000 coins[2][5]  

The San Francisco mint’s lower output makes the 1927-S particularly scarce in high grades[3][8]. Additionally, Philadelphia struck five experimental "Special Strike" nickels on chromium-plated dies, three of which are certified today[4]. These rare pieces, lacking official documentation, sold for up to $47,150 at auction[2][4].

## 2. Types, Categories, and Variations  

### Mint Marks and Rarity  
- **1927 (Philadelphia)**: The most common variety, with values ranging from $0.45 (Good) to $4,000 (MS 67)[2][3].  
- **1927-D (Denver)**: Scarcer in mint state, with MS 65 examples fetching $4,750 and an MS 66 specimen selling for $46,000 in 2005[2][8].  
- **1927-S (San Francisco)**: The rarest regular issue, with MS 63 grades valued at $2,677 and an MS 66 coin auctioned for $125,350 in 2008[2][7].  

### Error Coins and Special Strikes  
- **Two Feathers**: Missing the third feather on the Indian’s braid; a 1927-S example sold for $2,280 in 2021[2].  
- **3½ Legs**: A Denver-minted error where the bison’s front leg appears truncated. An MS 62 example commanded $7,475 in 2012[2].  
- **Special Strikes**: Philadelphia’s experimental 1927 issues, with one SP 65 coin realizing $47,150 in 2009[4].

## 3. Factors Determining Value  

### Grading and Condition  
Grading follows the Sheldon scale (1–70), with critical wear points on the Indian’s cheekbone and the bison’s horn and flank[1][3]:  
- **Good (G-4)**: Heavy wear, merged lettering; worth $1–$3[1][3].  
- **Extremely Fine (EF-40)**: Light abrasions, full date; valued at $9–$119[1][2].  
- **Uncirculated (MS-60+)**: No wear, original luster; MS 65 coins reach $400–$4,000, while MS 67 specimens hit $24,000[2][9].  

### Mint Marks and Survival Rates  
Denver and San Francisco issues are rarer due to lower mintages and circulation attrition. Only 5–10% of 1927-S nickels survive in grades above MS 60[7][8].

## 4. Identifying Authenticity and Quality  

### Key Authentication Features  
- **Mint Marks**: Located under "FIVE CENTS" on the reverse. Absence indicates Philadelphia[1][3].  
- **Design Details**: In uncirculated coins, the bison’s horn and Indian’s braid show sharp relief[5][9].  
- **Errors**: Reputable certification (PCGS, NGC) is essential for verifying rare varieties like the 3½ Legs[2][4].  

### Red Flags  
- **Artificial Toning**: Chemically induced patina appears uneven under magnification.  
- **Tooling Marks**: Scratches or re-engraved dates suggest alteration[6].

## 7. Preservation and Care  

### Best Practices  
- **Handling**: Use cotton gloves; avoid touching surfaces[6][9].  
- **Storage**: Acid-free flips or inert-gas capsules to prevent toning[6][11].  
- **Cleaning**: Never use abrasive methods. For encrusted dirt, a 24-hour soak in distilled water is safer than Worcestershire sauce or vinegar[10][12].  

### Professional Conservation  
NGC and PCGS offer conservation services to stabilize corroded or environmentally damaged coins without altering surfaces[4][9].
