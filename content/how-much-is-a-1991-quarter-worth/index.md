# The Comprehensive Guide to 1991 Washington Quarter Values

The 1991 Washington quarter, a staple of modern U.S. coinage, holds both historical significance and numismatic intrigue. ## Historical Context and Numismatic Significance

### Origins of the Washington Quarter Series
Introduced in 1932 to commemorate George Washington's bicentennial, the Washington quarter replaced the Standing Liberty design[2]. Sculptor John Flanagan's obverse portrait, based on Jean-Antoine Houdon's 1785 bust, became an enduring national symbol. The reverse's bald eagle design persisted until the 1999 State Quarters program, making the 1991 issue part of this 67-year design continuity[3][11].

The 1991 coins belong to the copper-nickel clad series initiated in 1965, containing a pure copper core sandwiched between outer layers of 75% copper and 25% nickel[1][13]. This composition shift from silver (90% pre-1965) fundamentally altered the series' metallurgical profile while maintaining its 5.67-gram weight and 24.3mm diameter[9][13].

### 1991 Minting Context
Three facilities produced 1991 quarters:
- **Philadelphia (No mint mark):** 570,968,000 struck[2][9]
- **Denver (D):** 630,966,693 struck[1][13]
- **San Francisco (S):** 2,867,787 proof specimens[2][9]

The combined 1.2+ billion mintage reflects the quarter's role as a workhorse of commerce during the early 1990s economic landscape[7][9]. However, survival rates in pristine condition remain surprisingly low due to extensive circulation, making high-grade examples numismatically significant[2][11].

## Grading and Authentication

### Critical Grading Criteria
- **Obverse:** Wear on Washington's cheekbone, hair details, and jawline
- **Reverse:** Eagle's breast feathers and talon definition
- **Luster:** Original cartwheel luster in uncirculated specimens[3][11]

PCGS and NGC population reports reveal stark rarity gradients:
- 1991-P MS67: ~115 known[2]
- 1991-D MS67: <50 confirmed[1]
- 1991-S PR70: 15-20 examples[9]

### Authentication Protocols
1. **Weight Verification:** 5.67g ±0.227g tolerance[9]
2. **Magnetic Testing:** Non-magnetic response (nickel content)[13]
3. **Edge Inspection:** 119 reeds ±5%[11]
4. **Professional Submission:** $30-$50 grading fees through NGC/PCGS[6][11]
