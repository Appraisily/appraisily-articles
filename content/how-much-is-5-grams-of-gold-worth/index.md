## Historical Background and Development of Gold Valuation  

### The Gold Standard and Early Valuation Systems  
Gold’s role as a store of value dates back millennia, but its formalized pricing emerged with the adoption of the gold standard. In 1900, the U.S. Gold Standard Act fixed gold at $20.67 per troy ounce[9], establishing a benchmark for monetary systems. This system collapsed during the Great Depression, leading to President Roosevelt’s Executive Order 6102 in 1933, which prohibited private gold ownership and reset the price to $35 per ounce[9]. These historical shifts underscore gold’s enduring economic importance and its transition from a currency backbone to a traded commodity.  

### Modern Price Volatility and Key Events  
The late 20th century saw dramatic price shifts. By 1980, gold peaked at $800 per ounce amid inflationary pressures, while the 2008 financial crisis drove prices to $1,873 per ounce by 2011[9]. For 0.5 grams, these macro trends translate to proportional value changes. For example, the 2025 price of $53.63 for 0.5 grams of 24k gold[1] reflects a 150% increase from 2000 levels, adjusted for inflation and market demand.

## Identifying Authentic 0.5-Gram Gold  

### Practical Authentication Tests  
- **Magnet Test**: Genuine gold is non-magnetic; attraction indicates iron or nickel alloys[10][14].  
- **Density Test**: Gold sinks in water due to high density (19.3 g/cm³). Floating suggests counterfeit materials[14].  
- **Acid Test**: Applying nitric acid to a scratch mark causes discoloration in impure gold[10].  

### Hallmarks and Certification  
Reputable products feature stamps like “999.9” or “24k” and come with assay cards (e.g., Valcambi bars[11]). The absence of these marks warrants caution.
