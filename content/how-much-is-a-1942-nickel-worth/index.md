# The Comprehensive Guide to the Value of 1942 Jefferson Nickels  

The 1942 Jefferson nickel occupies a unique position in American numismatic history, marking the transition from pre-war coinage to wartime composition. Its value stems from historical significance, compositional rarity, and collector demand. Depending on mint mark, condition, and type, 1942 nickels range from face value to over $30,000 for exceptional specimens. This guide explores the eight critical dimensions of these coins, providing collectors and historians with actionable insights grounded in archival data and market analysis.  

---

## Historical Context of the 1942 Jefferson Nickel  

### Wartime Composition Shift  
In 1942, the U.S. Mint altered the Jefferson nickel’s composition to conserve nickel for World War II armor production. The **Act of March 27, 1942**, authorized a new alloy of 56% copper, 35% silver, and 9% manganese[9][12]. This marked the first use of silver in five-cent coins since 1873[9]. Philadelphia produced both pre-war (Type 1) and wartime (Type 2) nickels in 1942, while Denver minted only Type 1 and San Francisco exclusively Type 2[7][10].  

### Minting Timeline and Production  
- **Pre-war (Type 1)**: Struck until October 1942 at Philadelphia (49,789,000) and Denver (13,938,000)[1][7].  
- **Wartime (Type 2)**: Philadelphia (57,873,000), San Francisco (32,900,000)[1][4].  
The Philadelphia Mint’s dual production created two distinct varieties: no-mint-mark Type 1 and large "P" mint mark Type 2[7][10].  

---

## Types and Variations of 1942 Nickels  

### Type 1: Pre-War Composition  
- **Alloy**: 75% copper, 25% nickel[3][9].  
- **Mint Marks**:  
  - **Philadelphia (No mint mark)**: 49.8 million struck[1].  
  - **Denver ("D")**: 13.9 million; mint mark located to Monticello’s right[7][12].  

### Type 2: Wartime Silver Nickels  
- **Alloy**: 35% silver, identifiable by large mint marks above Monticello[3][11].  
- **Mint Marks**:  
  - **Philadelphia ("P")**: First use of "P" on U.S. coins[10][12].  
  - **San Francisco ("S")**: 32.9 million struck[1][4].  

#### Notable Varieties  
- **1942-D/D Repunched Mint Mark**: Horizontal "D" overprint; sold for $32,200 in 2006[1].  
- **1942 Proofs**: Only 29,600 Type 1 and 27,600 Type 2 proofs; a Type 1 PR 68+ fetched $16,450 in 2023[1][3].

## Key Factors Influencing Value  

### 1. Composition and Silver Content  
Wartime nickels contain 1.75 grams of silver, giving them a melt value of $1.88 (as of April 2025)[6]. However, collector premiums often exceed bullion value.  

### 2. Mint Mark and Rarity  
- **Denver (D)**: Pre-war Type 1; MS 67+ examples reach $1,800[1].  
- **San Francisco (S)**: Wartime Type 2; MS 66 FS (Full Steps) sells for $210[4].  
- **Philadelphia (P)**: Type 2 MS 68 at $5,950[1].  

### 3. Condition and Grading  
Graded coins (MS 60–70) command exponential premiums:  
- **Circulated**: $0.05–$6[1][2].  
- **Uncirculated**:  
  - Type 1: $6–$225[1][2].  
  - Type 2: $7.50–$725[1][4].  

### 4. Errors and Varieties  
- **Full Steps (FS)**: Sharp stair details on Monticello; a 1942-S MS 66 FS sold for $210[4].  
- **Double Dies**: A 1942 DDO MS 65 fetched $765[1].

## Authentication and Grading  

### Identifying Wartime Nickels  
- **Mint Mark Placement**: Large "P," "D," or "S" above Monticello[3][11].  
- **Magnetism**: Silver-manganese alloy is non-magnetic[11].  
- **Weight**: 5 grams, consistent across types[6][12].  

### Professional Grading  
Services like **PCGS** and **NGC** authenticate and grade coins. A 1942 PR 68+ nickel certified by PCGS sold for $16,450[1][3].

## Preservation and Care Guidelines  

### Storage Best Practices  
- **Holders**: Use inert Mylar flips or NGC/PCGS slabs.  
- **Environment**: Store at 40–50% humidity, 65–70°F[3][12].  

### Handling Tips  
- Avoid cleaning; even mild abrasives reduce value[3][12].  
- Handle by edges to prevent obverse/reverse contact[10].
