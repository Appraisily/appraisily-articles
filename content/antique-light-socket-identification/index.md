### 1. **What is Antique Light Socket Identification?**  
Antique light socket identification involves analyzing historical electrical components to determine their age, manufacturer, design type, and authenticity. This practice is critical for:  
- **Restoration accuracy**: Matching original sockets preserves historical integrity[1][14].  
- **Valuation**: Rare or early sockets significantly increase a lamp’s value[16].  
- **Safety**: Identifying degraded materials (e.g., cloth wiring) ensures safe use[3][13].  

---

### 2. **Historical Background**  
- **1880s**: Thomas Edison’s first screw base used wood and plaster of paris, later replaced by brass and porcelain[2][15].  
- **1890s**: Westinghouse introduced push-in sockets with brass clips, competing with Edison’s screw design[8].  
- **1896**: General Electric standardized the modern Edison screw base (E26/E27), resolving insulation issues[2][15].  
- **1900–1910**: Pull-chain sockets (Hubbell, 1896) and wall plugs emerged, enabling household electrification[4][15].  

---

### 3. **Types and Variations**  
#### **By Design**  
| Type              | Description                                                                 | Example                     |  
|--------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------|  
| **Edison Screw**   | Threaded metal base; E12 (candelabra), E26 (standard)[6][7].               | 1881 plaster-insulated base[2] |  
| **Bayonet**        | Push-and-twist mechanism; common in Europe (B15, B22)[7].                  | Early 20th-century UK lamps |  
| **Westinghouse**   | Push-in sockets with brass clips; pre-1900 rarity[8].                      | 1890s solid brass socket[8] |  
| **Pull-Chain**     | Introduced by Harvey Hubbell (1896); acorn-shaped pulls[4][15].            | Tiffany-style lamps[15]     |  

#### **By Material**  
- **Brass/Copper**: Common in pre-1920s sockets[8][11].  
- **Porcelain**: Used for insulation in early 20th-century sockets[8].  
- **Bakelite**: Post-1920s plastic alternative[3].  

---

### 4. **Factors Determining Value**  
- **Age**: Pre-1900 sockets (e.g., Westinghouse brass) command premiums[8][16].  
- **Rarity**: Lange sockets (1890s) and transitional designs (1880–1881) are scarce[15].  
- **Condition**: Intact plaster insulation or original patina increases value[2][16].  
- **Provenance**: Sockets from makers like Tiffany Studios or Pairpoint add 30–50% value[11][16].  

---

### 5. **Authenticity Identification**  
- **Non-Polarized Plugs**: Pre-1970s plugs have equal-width prongs[5][9].  
- **Cloth-Covered Wiring**: Indicates pre-1940s manufacture[5][13].  
- **Maker’s Marks**: Look for stamps like "GE" or "Westinghouse" on sockets[11].  
- **Material Tests**: Authentic brass develops a mellow patina; reproductions often use lighter alloys[3][11].  

**Case Study**: A lamp with a porcelain Westinghouse socket (1890s) and cloth wiring sold for $450 at auction, while a reproduction with plastic insulation fetched $60[8][16].  

---

### 6. **Market Trends and Pricing**  
- **Entry-Level**: Common 1920s–1940s sockets (E26) sell for $10–$50[6][16].  
- **Mid-Range**: Early pull-chain or Art Deco designs: $100–$300[4][12].  
- **High-End**: Pre-1900 Westinghouse or Tiffany sockets: $500+[8][16].  

---

### 7. **Sourcing and Sales**  
- **Auctions**: Heritage Auctions and local estate sales for rare pieces[16].  
- **Specialists**: Retailers like Mosaic Shades and iRepairLamps offer authenticated sockets[1][4].  
- **Online**: Etsy and eBay list 10,000+ vintage sockets, but 40% are reproductions[5][9].  

---

### 8. **Preservation and Care**  
- **Cleaning**: Use mild soap on metal; avoid abrasives on porcelain[13].  
- **Wiring**: Replace frayed cloth cords with UL-rated kits[3][13].  
- **Storage**: Keep sockets in low-humidity environments to prevent corrosion[13].  
- **Bulbs**: Use ≤60W LEDs to avoid heat damage[13].
