## Historical Evolution of Gold Pennies  

### Medieval Origins and Economic Impact  
The gold penny emerged in 1257 under Henry III as England's first gold coinage, valued at 20 silver pence (1/12 pound sterling[5][9]). Struck from 23-karat gold (2.95g[5]), its design featured the king enthroned with orb and scepter[4]. However, flawed bimetallic valuation led to its withdrawal—gold's intrinsic value exceeded face value, prompting hoarding and melting[4][9]. Subsequent attempts, like Edward III’s 1344 florin, failed until the noble (1351) stabilized gold currency[4].

### Modern Adaptations: Commemorative and Plated Issues

## Authentication and Quality Assessment Protocols  

### Multistep Verification Process  
1. **Hallmark Analysis**: Medieval coins lack standardized stamps; rely on die comparisons (e.g., "HENRICUS REX III"[5]).  
2. **Metallurgical Testing**:  
   - XRF spectrometry confirms 23K purity in Henry III specimens[10].  
   - Nitric acid reactions differentiate plated zinc (green[14]) from solid gold (no reaction).  
3. **Numismatic Grading**: PCGS "Poor-1" to "MS-70" scale impacts 1943 Bronze Cent valuations by 500%[6].  

#### Red Flags of Counterfeits  
- Incorrect weight: Genuine Henry III pennies = 2.95g ±0.05g[5].  
- Anachronistic designs: Modern tooling marks on purported medieval issues.  
- Magnetism: Gold’s diamagnetic properties vs. ferrous alloys[14].
