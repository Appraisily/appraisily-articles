{{< content-modules/section-header title="History of Earth Stove" level="2" >}}
Understanding the history of Earth Stove helps put its value and collectibility into context. Founded in 1965 by Bob England, the company initially operated as England's Stove Works before evolving into the Earth Stove Corporation. They specialized in creating reliable wood-burning stoves and heating appliances that quickly gained popularity for their efficiency and durability.

Finding replacement parts is often critical for maintaining or restoring an Earth Stove to preserve its value. Here's where to look and what to consider.
{{< /content-modules/section-header >}}

![Earth Stove parts including door glass and hinges](https://ik.imagekit.io/appraisily/SEO/the-earth-stove-value/the-earth-stove-value-image-5_afxrgBrm9.png)

### Common Replacement Parts

1. **Door Glass** - Available from specialty retailers like [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Total-Hearth-102Arch-EA-12/dp/B0D8WQHYL4) with prices ranging from $65 to $105 depending on size and model
   
2. **Hardware Components** - Parts like the Hinge Pin Retrofit are available from [Lennox Hearth Parts](https://www.lennoxhearthparts.com/cgi/display.cgi?item_num=2800HT) for around $92

3. **Gaskets and Seals** - Critical for proper operation and preventing smoke leaks

4. **Handles and Knobs** - Often wear out or break with regular use

### Restoration Considerations

When restoring an Earth Stove:

1. **Original vs. Aftermarket** - Original parts maintain authenticity but quality aftermarket options may be more readily available

2. **Professional vs. DIY** - Complex repairs may require professional expertise, especially for safety-critical components

3. **Finish Restoration** - Proper high-temperature paint and preparation techniques are essential

4. **Cost vs. Value** - Consider whether restoration costs will be recovered in increased value

According to stove experts, maintaining original parts where possible helps preserve collector value, but safety should always be the priority when restoring a wood stove.

The growing interest in self-sufficiency and off-grid living has increased demand for reliable wood stoves, including vintage Earth Stoves. This trend has helped maintain steady values for well-maintained models.

Increasingly strict emissions regulations in many areas have created challenges for selling non-EPA certified wood stoves:

1. **Geographic Value Variations** - Earth Stoves may be worth more in rural areas with fewer restrictions
2. **Workshop/Outbuilding Use** - Many older Earth Stoves find new life in workshops and outbuildings where regulations may be less strict

A small but dedicated collector market exists for vintage wood stoves, including Earth Stoves:

{{< content-modules/faq title="Common Questions About Earth Stove Values" id="faq-section" >}}
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="What is an Earth Stove worth?" >}}
    Earth Stoves typically range in value from $50 to $300 depending on the model, condition, and local market. The most popular 100 Series models in good condition generally sell for $100-$200. Fully restored models with all original parts can sometimes fetch $300 or more. Models with damage, missing parts, or those that are non-functional may only be worth $50-$75, often for parts or restoration projects.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="How much is an old stove worth in scrap?" >}}
    If your Earth Stove is beyond repair or in very poor condition, it might be worth considering its scrap value. Typically, scrap yards pay between $10-$50 for appliances like stoves, depending on current metal pricing, the stove's size, weight, and condition. Cast iron components can be more valuable as scrap than sheet metal. However, even damaged Earth Stoves may be worth more when sold for parts to restorers than as scrap metal.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="What is the history of the Earth Stove?" >}}
    The Earth Stove was founded in 1965 by Bob England. The company initially operated as England's Stove Works before becoming the Earth Stove Corporation. They specialized in wood-burning stoves and heating appliances, quickly building a reputation for quality and reliability. In the 1990s, Lennox Industries acquired the company and continued producing the stoves. Earth Stoves became known for their efficient heating capacity and durable construction, developing a dedicated following among wood heat enthusiasts.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="Are Earth Stoves EPA certified?" >}}
    Most vintage Earth Stoves, particularly the popular 100 Series models, are not EPA certified by today's standards. They were manufactured before the strict emissions requirements currently in place. This lack of certification can affect their resale value and legality in certain areas with strict air quality regulations. These older, non-certified models are typically best suited for workshop or outbuilding use where regulations may be less restrictive, or in rural areas with fewer air quality restrictions.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}

  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="How can I tell which Earth Stove model I have?" >}}
    To identify your Earth Stove model:
    
    1. Look for an identification plate on the back of the stove, which typically contains the model number
    2. Check for distinctive design features - the 100 Series has recognizable door patterns
    3. Measure the stove's dimensions and compare to known specifications
    4. Consult the original owner's manual if available

    2. Intended use - restoration makes more sense if you plan to use the stove

For most owners and buyers, the real value of an Earth Stove lies in its utility rather than as an investment. A well-maintained Earth Stove can provide reliable heat for workshops, cabins, and outbuildings for years to come, often making them worth more in use than in resale value.
