{{< content-modules/section-header title="Dating Your Vintage Bulova Watch" level="2" >}}
One of the first steps in determining the value of an old Bulova watch is accurately identifying its age. Bulova used a specific dating system that changed over time, making it possible to determine when a watch was manufactured.
{{< /content-modules/section-header >}}

Bulova used several different date code systems throughout its history:

### Pre-1950s Watches
Watches produced before 1950 typically don't have standardized date codes. For these early models, dating relies primarily on:
- Case design and style elements
- Movement type and serial numbers
- Advertisements from the period
- Catalog references

### 1950-1969: The Letter-Number System
In 1950, Bulova introduced its first standardized date code system using a letter followed by a number:
- The letter (A through M, excluding I) represents the decade (L = 1950s, M = 1960s)
- The number (0-9) represents the specific year within that decade

For example, "L4" indicates 1954, while "M2" would be 1962.

### 1970-1980s: Two-Letter System
From 1970 to the early 1980s, Bulova switched to a two-letter system:
- The first letter (N through T) indicates the decade (N = 1970s)
- The second letter (A through M, excluding I) indicates the specific year

For example, "NJ" would represent 1979, while "PA" would indicate 1980.

### After 1980s
Dating becomes more complex after the 1980s, with various systems used depending on the watch model and production location.

### Bulova Accutron (1960-1977)
The Accutron represents Bulova's most significant technological achievement, utilizing a tuning fork mechanism that revolutionized accuracy in timekeeping. The most valuable Accutron models include:

- **Accutron Spaceview**: With its exposed tuning fork movement visible through the dial
- **Accutron Astronaut**: Designed for pilots and astronauts with GMT functionality
- **Accutron Snorkel**: Diving watches with distinctive cases and bezels
- **Accutron "TV" or "Cushion"**: With their distinctive 1970s-era case designs

### Bulova Marine Star (1940s-1960s)
These early waterproof models are highly sought after, particularly:
- Early 23-jewel automatic versions
- Models with original diver bezels intact
- Examples with original boxes and papers

### Bulova Chronographs (1940s-1970s)
Vintage Bulova chronographs, especially those with Valjoux movements, can command significant prices:
- **Bulova Chronograph C**: With distinctive tri-compax dial layout
- **Chronograph 23**: With internal rotating bezel models
- **"Stars and Stripes"**: Military-style chronographs from the late 1960s and early 1970s

### Art Deco Models (1920s-1930s)
Bulova's distinctive Art Deco designs from this era, particularly women's models, can be quite valuable:
- **Bulova "Lady Lindy"**: Named after Amelia Earhart
- **Diamond-set cocktail watches**: Especially those with unique case shapes
- **Men's "Banker" or "President"**: With distinctive rectangular cases

### Military-Issue Bulova (1940s-1950s)
Watches produced for the U.S. military during WWII and the Korean War era:
- **A-11 Specification watches**: With black dials and luminous hands
- **A-17 Navigation watches**: Larger 24-hour dials for military navigation
- Any watch with proper military markings and provenance

Understanding when and how to service or restore a vintage Bulova watch is crucial to maintaining or enhancing its value.
{{< /content-modules/section-header >}}

### To Restore or Preserve?
One of the most important decisions is whether to restore a watch or preserve its original condition:

- **For collectors' watches**: Original condition, even with visible aging, often preserves value
- **For everyday wear**: Careful restoration can make the watch more functional while maintaining value
- **For investment purposes**: Consult with specialists before any restoration work

### What Can Decrease Value
Some restoration practices can significantly reduce the value of vintage Bulova watches:

- **Dial refinishing**: Modern refinished dials are often obvious to collectors and can reduce value by 50%
- **Case polishing**: Aggressive polishing removes original finishes and case details
- **Non-original replacement parts**: Particularly hands, crowns, and movement components
- **Incorrect service methods**: Damaging movement components or altering original specifications

Not all watchmakers have experience with vintage Bulova watches. Look for:

- Membership in professional organizations like the American Watchmakers-Clockmakers Institute (AWCI)
- Specific experience with vintage American watches
- References from other collectors
- Portfolio of similar restoration work
- Clear communication about their restoration philosophy

### The Special Case of Bulova Accutron
Accutron models with tuning fork movements require specialized knowledge:

- Few watchmakers have experience with tuning fork technology

For Accutron models, it's essential to find a watchmaker with specific Accutron experience, as improper service can permanently damage these watches.
