Military buttons represent one of the most accessible yet historically significant artifacts that collectors can acquire. These small metal treasures have adorned military uniforms across centuries, each telling stories of battles fought, regiments disbanded, and nations transformed. For collectors and history enthusiasts, identifying rare military buttons can be both rewarding and challenging.

Military buttons serve both functional and symbolic purposes. Beyond securing uniforms, they identified the wearer's unit, rank, branch of service, and country of origin. The designs, materials, and construction methods have evolved considerably over time, making these elements crucial for identification.

![Close-up of military button showing detailed eagle design and attachment loop](https://ik.imagekit.io/appraisily/SEO/identification-rare-military-buttons/identification-rare-military-buttons-image-2_cIIDz3nMu.png)

### Basic Components of Military Buttons

Most military buttons share several common components:

1. **Face Design** - The front pattern, often featuring national emblems, regimental insignia, or branch-specific symbols
2. **Shank** - The attachment loop on the back of the button
3. **Backmark** - Manufacturer's mark stamped on the reverse
4. **Material** - Typically brass, pewter, silver, or gilt metals
5. **Size** - Generally ranging from 13mm to 25mm, with coat buttons being larger than cuff buttons

### Common Designs and Their Meanings

Military button designs aren't arbitrary—they carry specific meanings and affiliations:

- **Eagles**: Common on American military buttons, often with different poses representing different branches
- **Anchors**: Typically indicate naval service
- **Cannons/Artillery**: Associated with artillery units
- **State Seals**: Found on state militia buttons, particularly from the Civil War era
- **Numbered Buttons**: Display a unit or regiment number
- **Stars**: The 13-star pattern represents the original American colonies

### Button Construction

The way a button is manufactured offers significant clues to its age:

- **One-piece vs. Two-piece**: Earlier buttons (pre-1830s) were often one-piece cast construction, while later buttons typically have a face plate attached to a back plate
- **Shank Types**: Different attachment methods evolved over time:
  - Alpha shanks (early loop-style)
  - Omega shanks (U-shaped wire)
  - Sanders-type shanks (embedded wire)
  - Modern wire eye shanks

### Backmark Analysis

The manufacturer's mark on the back of the button is one of the most important dating tools:

![Close-up photograph of military button backmarks showing maker's marks and production dates](https://ik.imagekit.io/appraisily/SEO/identification-rare-military-buttons/identification-rare-military-buttons-image-3__dh0XLBtF.png)

Certain manufacturers operated during specific periods:
- **Scovill Manufacturing**: One of the most prolific button makers, with backmarks that evolved over time
- **Benedict & Burnham**: Common on Civil War era buttons
- **Waterbury Button Company**: Active from the 1800s to present
- **Albert, Scovill & Co.**: Used from 1827-1840
- **Horstmann & Co.**: Philadelphia-based maker of high-quality Civil War buttons

### Material Composition

Material analysis can provide crucial dating information:

- **Pewter**: Common in earliest buttons (Revolutionary War era)
- **Brass**: Became standard by the early 1800s
- **Silver-plated**: Often found on officer's buttons
- **Gilt**: Gold-washed brass, popular for dress uniforms

### Distinctive Markings and Symbols

Specific symbols help identify branch of service and time period:

- **Eagle designs**: American buttons feature different eagle poses for different branches
  - Spread eagle with shield (General Service)
  - Eagle on cannon (Artillery)
  - Eagle on anchor (Navy)

- **Letter designations**:
  - "I" for Infantry
  - "C" for Cavalry
  - "A" for Artillery

- **Special unit insignia**:
  - Bugle horns (Riflemen)
  - Crossed sabers (Cavalry)
  - Crossed cannons (Artillery)

### Civil War Confederate Buttons

Confederate buttons are among the most sought-after by collectors:

- **Confederate Officer Buttons**: CS buttons with state seals can command $500-$3,000+ depending on rarity and condition
- **Confederate Naval Buttons**: Extremely rare, often valued $1,000-$5,000
- **Confederate Local Buttons**: Made by Southern manufacturers during supply shortages, these crude but authentic buttons are highly collectible

### Revolutionary War Era Buttons

Among the oldest American military buttons:

- **Continental Army Buttons**: Simple designs with regiment numbers or state affiliations
- **Officer Buttons**: Often silver or silver-plated with more elaborate designs
- **State Militia Buttons**: Feature state symbols or mottos

### Specialty and Limited Issue Buttons

Some of the rarest buttons come from specialized units or limited production runs:

- **George Washington Inaugural Buttons**: Not military but highly prized by collectors, often $1,000+
- **Non-Regulation Officer Buttons**: Privately purchased by officers, often featuring unique designs
- **Foreign Legion Buttons**: French Foreign Legion and other international unit buttons
- **Elite Unit Buttons**: Buttons from elite or short-lived specialty units

![Rare Confederate military button with state seal design and tarnished brass finish](https://ik.imagekit.io/appraisily/SEO/identification-rare-military-buttons/identification-rare-military-buttons-image-4_MnUxOsrYC.png)

### Factors Affecting Button Value

Multiple factors influence the value of military buttons:

1. **Rarity**: Confederate, early American, and limited-issue buttons command premium prices
2. **Condition**: Surface detail, gilding retention, and shank integrity
3. **Historical Significance**: Buttons associated with famous battles or units
4. **Provenance**: Documented history of ownership or recovery location
5. **Completeness**: Original gilt or silver wash significantly increases value

### Visual Examination

Start with careful visual inspection:

- **Patina**: Authentic antique buttons develop natural patina that's difficult to replicate
- **Wear Patterns**: Genuine buttons show wear consistent with actual use on uniforms
- **Design Details**: Reproductions often lack the fine detail of originals
- **Manufacturing Marks**: Tool marks, casting flaws, or filing marks consistent with period production methods

### Maker's Marks Analysis

Study the backmarks carefully:

- **Font Style**: Typography of backmarks should match known examples from the period
- **Spelling**: Some manufacturers had consistent misspellings or unique spacing
- **Mark Placement**: Position and depth of the stamping can indicate authenticity
- **Evolution of Marks**: Many manufacturers changed their backmarks over time, helping with dating

### Material Testing

For serious collectors, scientific methods can confirm authenticity:

- **XRF Analysis**: X-ray fluorescence can determine metal composition
- **Microscopic Examination**: High-magnification reveals production methods
- **Patina Testing**: Chemical tests can determine if patina is natural or artificially applied

### Red Flags for Reproductions

Watch for these warning signs of fake buttons:

- **Too perfect**: Overly crisp details on supposedly old buttons
- **Incorrect weight**: Reproductions often feel lighter than originals
- **Modern production methods**: Machine-made uniformity in supposedly hand-crafted items
- **Artificial aging**: Chemical darkening that doesn't match natural aging patterns
- **Incorrect material composition**: Modern alloys that didn't exist during the period

### Condition Grading Scale

Military button condition is typically graded on this scale:

1. **Mint**: Perfect condition with original gilt or finish, sharp details, and intact shank
2. **Excellent**: Minor wear, 90%+ original finish, full details, intact shank
3. **Very Good**: Light wear, 70-90% finish, strong details, functioning shank
4. **Good**: Moderate wear, 40-70% finish, most details visible, shank may have minor issues
5. **Fair**: Heavy wear, minimal original finish, partially obscured details, shank may be damaged
6. **Poor**: Excessive wear, no original finish, major detail loss, damaged or missing shank

### Evaluating Specific Condition Issues

Pay particular attention to these common condition problems:

- **Shank Integrity**: Broken, repaired, or replaced shanks significantly reduce value
- **Surface Corrosion**: Pitting, verdigris, or excessive tarnish
- **Detail Preservation**: Wear to raised design elements
- **Gilt Retention**: Percentage of original gold wash remaining
- **Bends or Flattening**: Structural integrity of the button
- **Excavated Condition**: Soil damage, mineralization, or ground chemical effects

![Collection of various military buttons in different conditions showing wear patterns and patina](https://ik.imagekit.io/appraisily/SEO/identification-rare-military-buttons/identification-rare-military-buttons-image-5_RgSGLz41Q.png)

### Restoration Considerations

The collector market generally prefers:

- **Gentle Cleaning**: Light surface cleaning that preserves patina
- **Stabilization**: Halting active corrosion without altering appearance
- **Documentation**: Recording any conservation work performed

Avoid these value-destroying practices:
- Harsh cleaning that removes patina
- Buffing or polishing that erases surface details
- Shank repairs or replacements without disclosure
- Re-gilding or applying new finishes

### Storage Methods

Protect your collection with these storage techniques:

- **Individual Compartments**: Store each button separately to prevent contact damage
- **Acid-Free Materials**: Use acid-free paper, plastic sleeves, or cotton
- **Climate Control**: Maintain stable temperature and humidity levels
- **Display Options**: UV-protected display cases for buttons on exhibit

### Handling Best Practices

Proper handling preserves button condition:

- **Clean Hands**: Always handle with clean, dry hands or cotton gloves
- **Support the Button**: Hold by edges, not by shank or raised elements
- **Work Surface**: Use a padded work surface to prevent drops or impacts
- **Limited Handling**: Minimize unnecessary handling, especially of fragile specimens

### Documentation and Organization

Create a systematic approach to cataloging your collection:

- **Identification Records**: Document manufacturer, period, branch of service
- **Condition Reports**: Note existing damage, repairs, or conservation needs
- **Provenance Information**: Record any known history or recovery location
- **Photography**: Take high-resolution photos of front, back, and any special features
- **Valuation Updates**: Regularly update value estimates based on market trends

### Ethical Considerations

Responsible collecting practices include:

- **Legitimate Sources**: Purchase from reputable dealers with clear provenance
- **Archaeological Context**: Be cautious about buttons from undocumented digs that may destroy historical context
- **Disclosure**: When selling, disclose all known information about condition and repairs
- **Historical Preservation**: Consider donating significant finds to museums or educational institutions

{{< content-modules/faq title="Military Button FAQ" id="military-button-faq" >}}
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="What to do with old military buttons?" >}}
    Old military buttons can be:
    
    1. **Preserved in a collection** with proper storage and documentation
    2. **Donated to museums** if they have historical significance
    3. **Sold to reputable dealers** or at specialized militaria auctions
    4. **Repurposed into jewelry or crafts** (though collectors generally prefer preservation)
    5. **Researched and documented** to preserve their historical context
    
    For buttons with family significance, consider creating a shadow box display with information about the service member who wore them.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="What is the history of the 13 star button?" >}}
    The 13-star button design represents the original thirteen British colonies that formed the United States. This iconic pattern first appeared on military buttons in the early 19th century and became a standard motif on U.S. military buttons.
    
    The stars are typically arranged in a circular pattern surrounding a central design element like an eagle or anchor. This design symbolizes the foundation of the nation and has remained consistent on many U.S. military buttons despite uniform regulation changes over two centuries.
    
    The pattern is most commonly found on Navy buttons, but variations appear across different branches of service. Authentic 13-star buttons from the early to mid-19th century are valuable collector items.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="What were Civil War buttons made of?" >}}
    Civil War era buttons (1861-1865) were primarily made of brass, with some variations:
    
    - **Standard enlisted buttons**: Brass with various finishes
    - **Officer buttons**: Brass with gold gilt (wash)
    - **Confederate buttons**: Often brass, but shortages led to alternative materials like pewter, iron, wood, bone, and even ceramic
    - **Naval buttons**: Typically brass with more elaborate finishing
    
    Button construction typically involved a two-piece design with the face plate attached to a backing that held the shank. Manufacturing techniques varied between Northern and Southern producers, with Northern buttons generally showing higher quality and more consistent production standards.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="How can you tell if a military button is authentic?" >}}
    Authentication involves examining several key characteristics:
    
    1. **Material composition** - Period-appropriate metals and alloys
    2. **Manufacturing methods** - Production techniques matching the era
    3. **Patina development** - Natural aging versus artificial treatments
    4. **Backmark verification** - Manufacturer marks matching known historical examples
    5. **Design accuracy** - Precise details matching authenticated specimens
    6. **Weight and feel** - Appropriate heft for the type and period
    7. **Edge characteristics** - Production methods evident on button edges
    
    For valuable specimens, consider professional authentication or metallurgical testing. Compare suspected buttons with well-documented examples in museum collections or reference books.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="What is the most valuable military button?" >}}
    The most valuable military buttons generally fall into these categories:
    
    1. **Confederate Naval Officer buttons** - Can command $5,000+ in excellent condition
    2. **Rare Confederate state seal buttons** - Texas and South Carolina designs are particularly valuable
    3. **Revolutionary War regimental buttons** - Especially those with documented battlefield recovery
    4. **George Washington Inaugural buttons** - Not strictly military but highly prized by the same collectors
    5. **Extremely rare unit buttons** - Such as Confederate Submarine Service or specialized units
    
    The record prices for military buttons have exceeded $10,000 for the rarest Confederate naval examples with excellent provenance. Value is highly dependent on condition, authenticity, and documentation.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}
  
  {{< content-modules/faq-item question="How are military buttons dated?" >}}
    Military buttons can be dated through several methods:
    
    1. **Backmark Analysis** - Manufacturer marks changed over time and can be precisely dated
    2. **Construction Methods** - One-piece cast, two-piece assembled, shank types, etc.
    3. **Design Elements** - Motifs changed with uniform regulations
    4. **Material Composition** - Certain alloys were used in specific periods
    5. **Design Regulations** - Military regulations specified button designs at known dates
    
    For example, U.S. Army General Service buttons with the spread eagle changed subtly with each major uniform regulation (1821, 1851, 1872, 1902, etc.), allowing for precise dating based on these known design modifications.
  {{< /content-modules/faq-item >}}

### Essential Reference Books

These publications are considered essential for serious collectors:

1. **"American Military Button Makers and Dealers: Their Backmarks & Dates"** by William F. McGuinn and Bruce S. Bazelon
2. **"Record of American Uniform and Historical Buttons"** by Alphaeus H. Albert
3. **"Confederate States Navy and Marine Corps Uniforms, Equipment, and Buttons"** by Francis A. Lord
4. **"Uniform Buttons of the United States"** by Warren K. Tice
5. **"British Military Buttons in the American Revolution"** by Eric I. Goldstein and Stuart C. Mowbray

## Conclusion

Identifying and collecting rare military buttons offers a tangible connection to history through small but significant artifacts. These metal treasures served both practical and symbolic purposes, identifying the wearer's unit, rank, and allegiance while adorning uniforms with distinctive character.

For collectors, the journey of discovery involves careful examination of design elements, backmarks, materials, and construction techniques. Each button tells a story—whether it adorned a Revolutionary War soldier's coat, identified a Civil War regiment, or distinguished a 20th-century officer.

As with all antiques, authentication remains crucial in a market with increasing reproductions. Developing the expertise to distinguish genuine historical pieces from modern copies requires study, comparison, and sometimes scientific analysis. However, the reward of holding an authentic piece of military history makes the effort worthwhile.

Whether you're a beginning collector fascinated by these small artifacts or an advanced militaria specialist, military buttons offer an accessible and rewarding collecting field with specimens available across a wide range of price points. By preserving and documenting these items, collectors play an important role in maintaining connections to our military heritage.
