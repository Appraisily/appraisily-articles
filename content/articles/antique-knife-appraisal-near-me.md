### Key Value Factors

Professional appraisers consider multiple elements when determining a knife's value:

#### Age and Provenance

- **Manufacturing date** - True antiques are generally over 100 years old
- **Historical significance** - Association with important events or figures
- **Documentation** - Original papers, boxes, or proof of ownership
- **Chain of custody** - Traceable history of ownership

#### Maker and Manufacturing

- **Renowned makers** - Knives by famous craftsmen command premium prices
- **Manufacturing techniques** - Hand-forged vs. machine-made
- **Signature marks** - Maker's marks, factory stamps, and serial numbers
- **Regional characteristics** - Distinctive styles of different regions

As noted by [Classic Puma Knives](https://classicpumaknives.com/what-is-my-knife-worth/), "The most important aspect of determining price is the condition of the piece, especially the condition of the blade."

#### Condition Assessment

- **Blade condition** - Sharpness, patina, rust, or damage
- **Handle integrity** - Material quality and preservation
- **Mechanism functionality** - For folding knives, spring action
- **Originality** - Unaltered state with original components

### Documentation and Research

Before the appraisal:

1. **Gather any existing documentation** - Original receipts, certificates, previous appraisals
2. **Research the knife's history** - Family stories, acquisition details
3. **Identify any markings** - Take clear photos of maker's marks or stamps
4. **Create an inventory list** - Catalog each knife with basic descriptions

### Proper Handling and Presentation

When preparing the physical items:

1. **Clean appropriately** - Remove dust with a soft cloth, but avoid aggressive cleaning
2. **Stabilize damaged pieces** - Ensure loose parts are secure for transport
3. **Photograph thoroughly** - Take clear images from multiple angles
4. **Transport safely** - Use protective cases or wrapping for each knife

> "Never attempt to sharpen, polish, or restore an antique knife before appraisal. This can significantly reduce its value by destroying the original patina and historical integrity." â€” Bernard Levine, Knife Authentication Expert

### What Not to Do

Avoid these common mistakes:

- **Don't clean aggressively** - Harsh cleaning can remove valuable patina
- **Don't repair or restore** - Modifications can reduce authenticity and value
- **Don't sharpen blades** - Original edge condition is important to experts
- **Don't disassemble** - Keep all components together as originally assembled

Understanding what happens during a professional knife appraisal will help you make the most of the experience and ensure you receive accurate results.

### Initial Consultation

The process typically begins with:

1. **Discussion of your needs** - Insurance, selling, curiosity
2. **Overview of your collection** - General examination of what you have
3. **Explanation of the process** - How the appraiser will evaluate each piece
4. **Fee structure clarification** - Costs and payment expectations

### Detailed Examination

During the core appraisal:

1. **Visual inspection** - Careful examination of each knife
2. **Measurement and documentation** - Recording physical characteristics
3. **Identification of maker marks** - Analyzing stamps, etchings, and signatures
4. **Condition assessment** - Noting wear, damage, and preservation state
5. **Authentication verification** - Determining originality and age

Services like [Knife Purveyor](https://www.knifepurveyor.com/appraisal-services/) note, "Upon consignment of your knives, we will provide a free, updated appraisal of their current fair market value to reflect current demand."

### Value Determination

The appraiser will then:

1. **Research comparable sales** - Recent auction and private sale prices
2. **Consider market trends** - Current collector interests and demands
3. **Evaluate rarity factors** - Production numbers and availability
4. **Apply condition adjustments** - How condition affects standard values

### Final Documentation

At the conclusion:

1. **Written appraisal report** - Formal documentation of findings
2. **Value assessment** - Current market value and/or replacement value
3. **Authentication certificate** - For confirmed genuine pieces
4. **Recommendations** - Care, storage, or sales advice if requested

{{< content-modules/faq-item question="How old does a knife have to be to be an antique?" >}}
For a knife to be classified as a true antique, it generally needs to be at least 100 years old. This follows the standard definition used by customs agencies, auction houses, and collectors. As Wikipedia's knife collecting article states, "The knives of collectors may be antiques or even marketed as collectible. Antiques are knives at least 100 years old; collectible knives are of a later vintage than antique, and may even be new."

Knives that don't meet the 100-year threshold but are still of interest to collectors are often described as "vintage" or "collectible" rather than antique. For example, a knife from the 1950s would be considered vintage, while one from the 1850s would be a true antique.

Age alone doesn't determine value, however. A relatively recent custom knife by a renowned maker might be worth more than a much older mass-produced knife in poor condition.
