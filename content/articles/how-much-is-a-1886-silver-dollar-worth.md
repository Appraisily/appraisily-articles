The 1886 Morgan Silver Dollar is one of the most widely recognized and collected 19th-century American coins. With its iconic Liberty head design and status as a genuine silver coin from America's past, collectors and investors alike are often curious about its value. This comprehensive guide will help you understand what your 1886 silver dollar is worth, how to identify different varieties, and what factors influence its value in today's market.

{{< data-modules/stats-highlight title="Key Facts About the 1886 Morgan Dollar" columns="3" / /  >}}

- **Designer**: George T. Morgan
- **Composition**: 90% silver, 10% copper
- **Weight**: 26.73 grams
- **Silver Content**: 0.77344 troy ounces
- **Diameter**: 38.1 mm
- **Mintage**: Philadelphia (19,963,000), New Orleans (10,710,000), San Francisco (750,000)

The 1886 Morgan Silver Dollar features Lady Liberty's profile on the obverse and an eagle with spread wings on the reverse. These coins were minted at three different locations: Philadelphia (no mint mark), New Orleans (O mint mark), and San Francisco (S mint mark).

{{< content-modules/section-header title="Mint Mark Significance" level="2" >}}
The mint mark on your 1886 Morgan Silver Dollar is one of the most important factors determining its value. Let's examine each mint's production and how it affects the coin's worth today.
{{< /content-modules/section-header >}}

![1886 Morgan Silver Dollar showing mint mark location](https://ik.imagekit.io/appraisily/SEO/how-much-is-a-1886-silver-dollar-worth/how-much-is-a-1886-silver-dollar-worth-image-3_qHT2pkVnV.png)

### 1886 Philadelphia (No Mint Mark)

The Philadelphia Mint produced nearly 20 million Morgan dollars in 1886, making it the most common variety. These coins do not have a mint mark.

- **Mintage**: 19,963,000
- **Location of mint mark**: None (Philadelphia coins did not have mint marks)
- **Value range**: $34-$70 in circulated condition, $75-$400+ in uncirculated grades
- **Availability**: Common in most grades

### 1886-O New Orleans

The New Orleans mint produced over 10 million coins, but far fewer have survived in uncirculated condition.

- **Mintage**: 10,710,000
- **Location of mint mark**: On reverse below the wreath, above "DOLLAR"
- **Value range**: $50-$150 in circulated condition, $600-$1,400+ in uncirculated
- **Availability**: Common in circulated grades, scarce in uncirculated

### 1886-S San Francisco

The San Francisco mint had the lowest production of 1886 Morgan dollars, making these the scarcest of the three varieties.

- **Mintage**: 750,000
- **Location of mint mark**: On reverse below the wreath, above "DOLLAR"
- **Value range**: $150-$400 in circulated condition, $900-$1,700+ in uncirculated
- **Availability**: Scarce in all grades, especially uncirculated

### Common Coin Grades and Their Meaning

- **Circulated Grades (G-4 to AU-58)**
  - Good (G-4 to VG-8): Heavy wear, major details visible
  - Fine (F-12 to VF-30): Moderate wear, most details visible
  - Extremely Fine (EF/XF-40 to AU-58): Light wear, almost all details sharp

- **Uncirculated Grades (MS-60 to MS-70)**
  - MS-60 to MS-62: No wear but may have many contact marks
  - MS-63 to MS-64: Fewer marks, better eye appeal
  - MS-65 and higher: Exceptional quality with minimal imperfections

For 1886 Morgan dollars, the jump in value between circulated and uncirculated conditions can be substantial, especially for the New Orleans and San Francisco mint coins.

{{< content-modules/section-header title="Rare Varieties and Errors" level="2" >}}
While standard 1886 Morgan dollars are relatively common, certain varieties and errors can command significant premiums. Here are some noteworthy examples to look for:
{{< /content-modules/section-header >}}

### Doubled Die Varieties

Some 1886 Morgan dollars show doubling in certain parts of the design. Look for doubling in the date, inscriptions, or other design elements.

### VAM Varieties

VAM varieties (named after researchers Van Allen and Mallis) are specific die variations that collectors actively seek. Notable 1886 VAMs include:

- **VAM-1A (Doubled Date)**: Shows doubling in the date area
- **VAM-17 (Doubled Arrows)**: Displays doubling on the arrow feathers
- **VAM-21 (Doubled Stars)**: Features doubling on some of the stars

### Error Coins

Various production errors can significantly increase the value of an 1886 Morgan dollar:

- **Off-center strikes**: When the coin is struck outside the normal centered position
- **Die breaks and cracks**: Resulting in raised lines on the coin
- **Clashed dies**: When dies strike each other without a planchet between them, transferring design elements

### Silver Content Value

Each 1886 Morgan Silver Dollar contains 0.77344 troy ounces of pure silver. This means the coin has an intrinsic "melt value" based on the current silver spot price. Even the most common 1886 Morgan dollars are typically worth more than their silver content due to their numismatic (collector) value.

At a silver price of $30 per ounce, the intrinsic silver value would be approximately $23.20. However, virtually all 1886 Morgan dollars sell for a premium above this melt value.

### Collecting Strategies

1. **Mint Mark Set**: Acquire one example from each mint (Philadelphia, New Orleans, and San Francisco)

2. **Condition-Based Collection**: Focus on obtaining the highest quality specimens within your budget

3. **VAM Variety Collection**: Specialize in the different VAM varieties that exist for 1886

4. **Complete Date Set**: Include the 1886 as part of a larger Morgan dollar collection spanning multiple years

5. **Toning Specialization**: Some collectors focus on attractively toned specimens with natural color development

### Authenticity Concerns

When collecting valuable coins like the 1886 Morgan dollar, authenticity is paramount. Here are some red flags that might indicate a counterfeit:

- Incorrect weight (genuine coins weigh 26.73 grams)
- Poor detail reproduction, especially in Lady Liberty's hair or the eagle's feathers
- Incorrect metal composition (should be 90% silver)
- Unusual mint marks or irregularities in the date

For valuable specimens, professional authentication through third-party grading services like PCGS or NGC is highly recommended.

## Conclusion

The 1886 Morgan Silver Dollar represents an important piece of American numismatic history. While Philadelphia-minted specimens are relatively common and affordable in circulated grades, the New Orleans and San Francisco issues can command significant premiums, especially in uncirculated condition.

Before buying or selling an 1886 Morgan dollar, take the time to properly identify its mint mark and assess its condition. For valuable specimens, professional grading is highly recommended to authenticate the coin and establish its true market value.

Whether you're a seasoned collector or someone who just discovered an 1886 silver dollar in a family collection, understanding these factors will help you appreciate the true value of these historic silver coins. With proper care and handling, these pieces of American history can continue to be preserved and cherished for generations to come.
