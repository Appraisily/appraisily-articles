## 1. The Fundamentals of Old Vise Identification  

Old vise identification involves analyzing physical characteristics, manufacturing marks, and historical context to determine a vise’s age, origin, and authenticity. This process is valuable for three reasons:  

1. **Historical Preservation**: Vises from the 19th and early 20th centuries reflect industrial manufacturing techniques, such as hand-forged wrought iron bodies (pre-1850) versus later cast iron designs [12]. For example, Prentiss Vise Co.’s 1868 patent for swiveling jaws marked a shift toward mass-produced industrial tools [2].  
2. **Collector Value**: Rare models like the Wilton Bullet vise (1941–present) command prices exceeding $3,000 due to their precision engineering and wartime-era manufacturing [14].

## 2. Historical Evolution of Vise Identification  

### Pre-Industrial Era (Pre-1830)  
Early vises were wooden clamping devices used by blacksmiths and carpenters. The first cast iron vise emerged in 1830, though air pockets in early castings led to fragile designs [12].  

### Industrial Revolution (1830–1900)  
Manufacturers like Prentiss Vise Co. (founded 1868) and Wilton Tool Corp. (1941) standardized production. Key innovations included:  
- **Swivel bases**: Patented by Mason Prentiss in 1868 to accommodate tapered workpieces [2].  
- **Quick-release mechanisms**: Introduced in the 1850s to reduce manual screw adjustments [9].  

### Modern Era (Post-1941)  
Wilton’s 1941 bullet vise, with its sealed screw mechanism, became a benchmark for durability. Date-stamped slide keys (e.g., “9-50” for September 1950) allowed precise dating until 1954, when “GUAR EXP” stamps indicated warranty expiration dates instead [1].

## 3. Types and Variations of Antique Vises  

### Bench Vises  
- **Machinist Vises**: Feature precision-ground jaws (±0.001” tolerance) and hardened steel inserts. The 1948 Wilton 930 MC (3” jaws) is a prime example [14].  
- **Combination Vises**: Include pipe jaws for cylindrical work. A 1930s Reed 204R with intact pipe grips sells for $800–$1,200 [4].  

### Leg Vises  
Blacksmithing leg vises, such as Peter Wright’s 19th-century models, have a vertical leg stabilizing the vise during heavy hammering. Authentic examples show hand-forged chamfers and “P.WRIGHT PATENT SOLID BOX” stamps [10].  

### Specialty Vises  
- **Coachmaker’s Vises**: Wide jaws (up to 12”) for woodworking.  
- **Post Vises**: Mounted on vertical beams in railroad workshops, identifiable by reinforced screw boxes [8].
