## Historical Evolution of Tool Identification Practices  

### Pre-Industrial Identification Methods  
Before standardized manufacturing, tool identification relied on oral traditions and apprenticeship knowledge. Blacksmiths and guild craftsmen maintained mental catalogs of regional tool designs, recognizing variations in hammer head shapes or saw tooth patterns that indicated specific trades or geographical origins. The 18th-century Enlightenment brought systematic classification, with Denis Diderot's *Encyclopédie* (1751-1772) documenting over 200 tool types through detailed engravings, establishing foundational typologies still referenced today[3].  

### Industrialization's Impact on Tool Traceability  
Mass production techniques introduced by firms like Stanley Works (1843) and Millers Falls (1868) necessitated stamped identifiers, patent numbers, and catalogs. The 1842 British Registered Design system created verifiable paper trails, while U.S. Patent Office records after 1836 provide precise dating through utility patents[2][5]. Case Study: A 1903 Millers Falls eggbeater drill (#45 model) can be authenticated through cross-referencing catalog illustrations with the company's 1896-1910 production records, verifying its transitional design features[4].
