## 2. Historical Evolution of Wooden Rocking Horses  

### Early Origins and Medieval Precursors  
The rocking horse’s lineage traces to medieval hobby horses—simple stick-mounted toys used for mock jousting. By the 17th century, bow rockers emerged, featuring curved wooden bases that allowed back-and-forth motion. One of the earliest surviving examples, attributed to King Charles I, was designed to strengthen his legs during childhood[2]. These early models were carved from solid wood, making them prone to tipping, a flaw addressed in the Victorian era through hollow construction and safety stands[1].  

### Victorian Innovations and Industrialization  
The 19th century saw transformative advancements, notably Philip Marqua’s 1880 safety stand, which replaced bow rockers with a stable gliding frame[12]. Manufacturers like F.H. Ayres and Lines Brothers adopted this design, producing horses with dappled gray paint, glass eyes, and horsehair manes[3][6]. The Industrial Revolution enabled partial mass production, though carving remained manual. World Wars I and II disrupted production, leading to a decline in traditional makers[1], but post-war nostalgia revived interest, culminating in modern artisans like Sally’s Rocking Horses preserving these techniques[7].

## 3. Types and Variations of Antique Rocking Horses  

### Bow Rockers vs. Safety Stands  
- **Bow Rockers**: The earliest form, dating to the 17th century, features curved wooden runners. These are rarer and often less stable, with values ranging from $1,250 for 19th-century examples[10] to $6,000 for pristine models[1].  
- **Marqua Safety Stands**: Introduced in 1880, these gliding frames offered stability and space efficiency. A restored Lines Brothers Edwardian horse on a safety stand sold for $3,000[1], while Haddon Rockers’ models fetch $1,850–$2,950[16].  

### Material and Maker Variations  
- **Wooden Horses**: High-value examples use mahogany or pine, with leather tack and horsehair details. An 1860s European pine rocker recently auctioned for $3,136[10].  
- **Metal and Tin**: Mass-produced in the mid-20th century, these are less desirable, often priced under $50[1].  
- **Regional Styles**: English makers like Collinson emphasized dappled gray paint and square pillars[5], whereas American firms like Marqua prioritized glider mechanisms[12].
