Old bottle identification combines historical research, material analysis, and collector knowledge to determine the age, origin, and value of vintage glass containers. This guide synthesizes expertise from antique specialists and market data to provide a comprehensive resource.

## 1. Fundamentals of Old Bottle Identification  
**Definition**: The process of analyzing physical characteristics (shape, glass composition, manufacturing marks) to establish a bottle's production era (typically pre-1950), original purpose, and cultural context[1][4].  

**Value Drivers**:  
- **Historical significance**: Bottles from prohibition-era speakeasies or early pharmaceutical companies often carry premium values[4][6]  
- **Rarity**: Only 12% of pre-1900 embossed medicinal bottles remain intact according to auction records[3]  
- **Aesthetic appeal**: Cobalt blue poison bottles regularly sell for $800-$1,200 at specialty auctions[6]  

## 2. Historical Development Timeline  
| Era | Key Innovation | Identification Marker |  
|------|----------------|-----------------------|  
| Pre-1840 | Free-blown glass | Irregular pontil marks[2] |  
| 1840-1900 | Mold-blown production | Seam lines stopping below lip[1] |  
| 1900-1920 | Automatic bottle machines | Full-height vertical seams[4] |  
| Post-1920 | Mass production | Standardized measurements[2] |  

The 1884 Owen's suction mold invention revolutionized bottle uniformity, making post-1900 specimens easier to date through catalog comparisons[1][2].

## 3. Primary Classification Categories  
**By Function**:  
- **Medicinal**: Square shoulders, embossed dosages (e.g., "One Teaspoonful")[3]  
- **Beverage**: Thick glass with trademark embossing (Coca-Cola hobble-skirt design)[6]  
- **Household**: Wide-mouth canning jars with zinc lids[1]  
- **Specialty**: Figural perfume bottles with hand-painted details[3]  

**By Manufacturing Technique**:  
- **Dip molds**: Crude asymmetrical bases (pre-1860)  
- **Turn molds**: Swirled glass patterns (1870-1910)  
- **Press-and-blow**: Uniform thickness (post-1905)[2]  

## 4. Valuation Factors  
**Condition Grading Scale**:  
| Grade | Characteristics | Value Multiplier |  
|-------|-----------------|------------------|  
| Mint | No wear, original label | 3.5x base |  
| Excellent | Minor surface scratches | 2x base |  
| Good | Stable discoloration | 1x |  
| Poor | Chips/cracks | 0.3x[4] |  

Color rarity significantly impacts value:  
- **Common**: Clear, amber (35% of market)  
- **Mid-tier**: Green, cobalt (55%)  
- **Premium**: Sun-colored amethyst, red (10%)[4][6]  

## 5. Authentication Techniques  
**Three-Step Verification Process**:  
1. **Base examination**:  
   - Open pontil marks = pre-1855[2]  
   - Machine-made concentric circles = post-1910[4]  
2. **Lip analysis**:  
   - Applied collars = hand-finished (pre-1890)  
   - Fire-polished = machine-made[1]  
3. **Embossing comparison**:  
   - Crude lettering with raised dots = 19th century[2]  
   - Precise logos = post-1920s[6]  

Case Study: A 1880s "Hostetter's Stomach Bitters" bottle was authenticated through its crude embossing, iron pontil mark, and period-appropriate olive green glass[3][4].

## 8. Preservation Protocols  
**Cleaning Hierarchy**:  
1. **Basic maintenance**:  
   - Lukewarm water + pH-neutral soap[10]  
   - Soft bristle brushes (never steel wool)[11]  
2. **Stain removal**:  
   - 24hr vinegar soak for mineral deposits[9]  
   - Denture tablets for organic residue[10]  
3. **Professional restoration**:  
   - Rotary tumble polishing ($25-$75/bottle)[10]  

**Storage Guidelines**:  
- Relative humidity: 45-55%[12]  
- UV-filtered display lighting[11]  
- Acid-free tissue padding for rare labels[12]
