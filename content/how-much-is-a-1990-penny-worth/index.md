## 2. Historical Development of the 1990 Penny  

### Transition to Zinc-Core Composition  
In response to rising copper costs, the U.S. Mint adopted a 97.5% zinc and 2.5% copper composition in 1982[8]. By 1990, this formula was standardized, reducing production costs to 0.8 cents per penny while maintaining the coin’s iconic appearance[9]. The 1990 issue thus reflects a mature phase of this metallurgical shift, with consistent weight (2.5g) and diameter (19.05mm)[7].  

### Mintage and Mint Mark Distribution  
- **Philadelphia (No Mint Mark)**: Struck 6.85 billion pennies, the highest volume, with MS 69 RD specimens now valued at $7,500[1][4].  
- **Denver (D Mint Mark)**: Produced 4.92 billion coins; MS 69 RD examples reach $3,000[1][7].  
- **San Francisco (S Mint Mark)**: Minted 3.3 million proof coins, including the rare No-S error[6][7].  

The accidental omission of the S mint mark on ~3,055 proof dies in 1990 created a numismatic anomaly, as San Francisco had not produced circulation pennies since 1974[2][6].
