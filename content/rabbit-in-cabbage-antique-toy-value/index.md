## Historical Background and Development  

### Origins of Roullet & Decamps  
The rabbit in cabbage automaton was manufactured by Roullet & Decamps, a Parisian firm founded in 1865 by Jean Roullet. The company gained prominence after Ernest Decamps, a skilled mechanic, married Roullet’s daughter in 1879, leading to the formal establishment of Roullet & Decamps in 1889[7][10]. Specializing in whimsical automata, the firm produced mechanical animals such as violin-playing rabbits, smoking monkeys, and the iconic rabbit in cabbage. These creations were designed to entertain bourgeois families during an era when clockwork novelties symbolized technological sophistication and social status[1][10].  

### Mechanical and Cultural Significance  
The automaton’s mechanism—a combination of gears, cams, and springs—allowed the rabbit to perform a lifelike sequence: rising from the cabbage, turning its head, chewing a leaf, and retreating abruptly. A built-in musical movement often accompanied the performance, enhancing its theatrical appeal[12][13]. The design capitalized on the Victorian fascination with anthropomorphic animals, as seen in contemporary literature like Beatrix Potter’s *Peter Rabbit* (1902), which popularized the trope of rabbits in gardens[12]. By blending technical precision with narrative charm, Roullet & Decamps secured the rabbit in cabbage’s status as a bestseller, with production continuing into the 1930s[6][9].

## Authentication and Identification  

### Hallmarks of Authenticity  
- **Manufacturer’s Mark**: Look for the “Roullet & Decamps” stamp or “RD” key[1][9].  
- **Construction Materials**: Authentic models use brass gears, steel springs, and hand-stitched fabric leaves[7][12].  
- **Movement Complexity**: Genuine automatons have a multi-step sequence (rising, chewing, retreating) driven by a single spring[13].  

### Common Frauds  
- **Reproductions**: Modern replicas often lack the RD key or use plastic components.  
- **Cannibalized Parts**: Some sellers combine parts from damaged automatons; mismatched wear patterns (e.g., new fur on faded cabbage) indicate tampering[12][16].
