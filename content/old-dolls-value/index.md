## 2. Historical Evolution of Doll Valuation  

### Early Origins (Pre-1800s)  
Dolls initially served ritualistic or educational purposes. Egyptian paddle dolls (2000 BCE) and Roman ivory figurines were among the earliest forms. By the 16th century, German wooden *docken* emerged as playthings for affluent European children[16].  

### Industrialization and Mass Production (19th Century)  
The 1850s marked a turning point with **French bisque dolls** from manufacturers like Jumeau and Bru. These dolls featured lifelike glass eyes, human-hair wigs, and articulated bodies, fetching prices equivalent to a worker’s annual wage[3][17]. German firms like Armand Marseille later dominated the market, producing over 1,000 bisque head molds by 1900[5][12].  

### 20th-Century Shifts  
- **1920s–1940s**: Composition dolls (sawdust and glue) replaced fragile bisque, exemplified by Shirley Temple dolls (1934–1962), which now sell for $300–$1,500 depending on condition[3][17].  
- **1950s–1970s**: Vinyl and plastic enabled mass production, with Barbie (1959) and Madame Alexander’s collectibles leading the market[4][14].

## 5. Authenticity Assessment Techniques  

### Step-by-Step Identification  
1. **Manufacturer Marks**: Check neck, shoulder, or foot stamps. For example, “A.M.” indicates Armand Marseille[5][17].  
2. **Material Analysis**: Use UV light to detect modern repairs on bisque; original 19th-century porcelain fluoresces differently[8][12].  
3. **Clothing and Accessories**: Hand-stitched linen undergarments vs. synthetic reproductions[3][8].  

### Red Flags  
- **Repainted Faces**: Brushstrokes inconsistent with era-specific techniques (e.g., airbrushing on pre-1920 dolls)[5].  
- **Non-Original Bodies**: Composition dolls with vinyl limbs post-1950[4][17].

## 8. Preservation Best Practices  

### Storage Guidelines  
- **Environment**: Maintain 40–50% humidity, 18–22°C (64–72°F) to prevent material degradation[8][10].  
- **Lighting**: UV-filtered cases reduce fabric fading; incandescent bulbs preferred over LEDs[8][10].  

### Cleaning Protocols  
- **Bisque/Porcelain**: Soft brush dusting; avoid water to prevent glaze damage[8][10].  
- **Cloth Dolls**: Spot-clean with distilled water and pH-neutral soap[8][17].  

### Restoration Ethics  
- **Document Interventions**: Professional conservators recommend photographing repairs for transparency[5][12].  
- **Avoid Over-Restoration**: Retaining original patina (e.g., mild crazing on bisque) preserves historical integrity[8][10].
