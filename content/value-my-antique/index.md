Antiques serve as tangible connections to history, embodying cultural, artistic, and material significance. This guide explores the multifaceted nature of antique valuation, tracing its historical roots, examining key factors influencing worth, and providing practical advice for preservation and sale. ## 1. Defining Antique Valuation and Its Importance  

Antique valuation is the systematic process of determining the monetary and historical worth of objects typically over 100 years old[5][6]. This practice is essential for several reasons:  

- **Insurance and Probate**: Accurate valuations ensure proper coverage for loss or damage and facilitate equitable distribution of estates[2][6].  
- **Sales and Auctions**: Sellers rely on appraisals to set realistic prices, while buyers use them to verify authenticity and investment potential[1][8].  
- **Historical Preservation**: Valuations often uncover an item’s provenance, linking it to specific eras, artisans, or historical events[9][10].  

For example, a 17th-century French Verdure tapestry’s value skyrockets if tied to the Gobelins manufactory, reflecting both craftsmanship and historical context[12]. Without formal appraisal, owners risk undervaluing heirlooms or overpaying for reproductions.

## 2. Historical Evolution of Antique Valuation  

The practice of valuing antiques dates to the Renaissance, when collectors began preserving classical artifacts. However, formal appraisal systems emerged in the 18th century alongside Europe’s auction houses, such as Christie’s (1766) and Sotheby’s (1744)[6][9]. These institutions standardized methods for assessing age, provenance, and condition, setting precedents still used today.  

The 20th century saw the rise of professional appraisal organizations, including the British Antique Dealers’ Association (BADA), which codified ethical guidelines and valuation criteria[6]. Technological advancements, such as online platforms like Mearto[5] and Bidsee[11], have democratized access to appraisals, enabling global participation in antique markets.

## 5. Identifying Authentic Antiques  

### 5.1 Material Analysis  
- **Wood**: Growth rings and tool marks indicate pre-20th-century construction[3].  
- **Metals**: Hallmarks on silver or gold items verify origin and date[4][6].  

### 5.2 Provenance Research  
A painting’s ownership history, documented via bills of sale or exhibition records, can elevate its value. For instance, a portrait once owned by royalty may fetch 300% more than an identical unsigned work[9][10].  

### 5.3 Expert Appraisal
