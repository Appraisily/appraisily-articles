## 2. Historical Evolution of Glass Bottle Production and Collectibility  

### 2.1 Ancient Origins to Industrial Revolution  
The earliest glass bottles emerged in Mesopotamia circa 1500 BCE, crafted using core-forming techniques that produced thick-walled vessels for perfumes and oils[11][15]. Roman glassblowers revolutionized production in the 1st century BCE, creating standardized wine amphorae with pontil-marked bases—a feature still used to authenticate ancient specimens[2][14]. The Middle Ages saw limited production due to glassmaking guild restrictions, but by the 1600s, English and German factories were producing utilitarian bottles for spirits and medicines[11].  

The 1860 invention of the automatic bottle machine by Philip Arbogast marked a turning point, enabling mass production. This technological leap is evident in the price disparity between pre-1860 hand-blown medicinal bottles (averaging $300–$800) and their machine-made counterparts ($50–$200)[6][9].  

### 2.2 The Golden Age of Bottle Manufacturing (1880–1915)  
This period saw explosive innovation, including:  
- **Mason jars** (1858): Patented by John Landis Mason, these food preservation vessels now command $150–$400 for rare amber variants[2][7].  
- **Hutchinson soda bottles**: Early 1900s examples with regional brewery embossing sell for $75–$250[3][13].  
- **Poison bottles**: Cobalt or emerald glass bottles with textured surfaces to aid tactile identification; values range from $200 to $1,200[9][13].

## 5. Authentication Techniques for Collectors  

### 5.1 Material Analysis  
- **Ultraviolet testing**: Uranium glass emits green fluorescence under UV light (365 nm)[4][7].  
- **Pontil marks**: Open pontils (pre-1850) vs. snap pontils (1850–1880) vs. smooth bases (post-1880)[10][14].  

### 5.2 Manufacturing Clues  
- **Mold seams**: Early 3-piece molds leave vertical lines stopping below the neck; ABM (automatic bottle machine) seams extend to the lip[3][10].  
- **Lip formation**: Hand-applied lips show irregular tooling marks; machine-made lips are uniform[14].  

A 2023 case study demonstrated these methods: A purported 18th-century onion bottle was revealed as a 1970s reproduction due to modern annealing lines and lack of proper basal wear[10][13].
