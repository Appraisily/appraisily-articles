# The Comprehensive Guide to Understanding and Valuing Old Military Buttons  

Old military buttons serve as tangible connections to historical conflicts, organizational pride, and evolving craftsmanship. Their value stems from historical significance, material composition, rarity, and collector demand. This report synthesizes research from auction records, historical archives, and expert analyses to provide a detailed examination of military button valuation, authentication, and preservation.  

## Historical Evolution of Military Buttons  

### Early Origins and Functional Beginnings  
Military buttons emerged in the 18th century as functional fasteners, but their decorative potential quickly became apparent. The **Continental Army** during the American Revolution used buttons featuring intertwined "US" and "A" designs, symbolizing unity and rank[9][13]. By the **War of 1812**, pewter buttons with regiment-specific markings (e.g., "2R" for Second Regiment) became standardized, though their fragility limited survival rates[2][4]. The **Civil War** era marked a turning point, with brass buttons manufactured by firms like **Scovill** and **Waterbury Button Company** dominating Union uniforms, while Confederate forces relied on imported or repurposed designs[10][14].  

### Technological Advancements and Standardization  
The **two-piece convex button**, patented in 1813, revolutionized production by combining a stamped front shell with a brazed backplate[4]. By the 1830s, **staff buttons** with intricate "spread eagle" motifs became synonymous with U.S. Army officers, a design retained until 1902[13]. World War II introduced plastic buttons to conserve metals, though brass remained prevalent for dress uniforms[3]. Modern military buttons, such as the **Navy’s foul anchor design** (adjusted in 1941 to comply with heraldic standards), reflect centuries of iterative refinement[3][9].

## Classification and Typology of Military Buttons  

### By Historical Period  
- **Revolutionary War (1775–1783):** Hand-cast pewter or brass buttons with crude shanks; rare surviving examples like the **Continental Army cuff button** fetch $500–$1,000+[2][11].  
- **Civil War (1861–1865):** Union buttons with **raised eagle motifs** and maker marks (e.g., "Horstmann Philadelphia") versus Confederate variants with cruder craftsmanship; values range from $60 (common infantry) to $2,000+ (rare artillery or cavalry)[14][10].  
- **World Wars (1914–1945):** Transition to **tinned iron backs (TIB)** and synthetic materials; unit-specific designs like the **Marine Corps’ globe-and-anchor** command premiums[3][9].  

### By Military Branch  
- **Army:** The **Great Seal button**, adopted in 1902, remains standard, though pre-1902 "spread eagle" staff buttons are highly collectible[13].  
- **Navy:** Post-1941 buttons feature eagles facing right per heraldic rules; pre-1941 left-facing examples are scarcer[3].  
- **Marine Corps:** Stable designs since 1821, with gilt for dress uniforms and bronze for service attire[3].  

### By Construction  
- **One-Piece Cast Buttons:** Common pre-1813; identified by integral shanks and uneven edges[4][9].  
- **Two-Piece Crimped Buttons:** Post-1813 innovation with soldered backplates; prevalent in Civil War-era finds[4][14].  
- **Staff Buttons (1830s–1902):** Hollow, gold-gilt buttons with a crimped rim; officers’ versions exhibit higher relief[13].

## Determinants of Value and Authenticity  

### Key Valuation Factors  
1. **Rarity:** Confederate "blockade runner" buttons, produced in limited quantities, often exceed $1,000[10][11]. Conversely, mass-produced WWII plastic buttons rarely surpass $10[3].  
2. **Condition:** A **pristine Civil War staff button** with intact gilt may fetch $300, while a corroded counterpart sells for $50[14].  
3. **Provenance:** Buttons excavated from battlefields (e.g., **Gettysburg**) or linked to historical figures (e.g., **Robert E. Lee**) carry 20–50% premiums[13][14].  
4. **Back Marks:** Maker-specific stamps like "Waterbury Button Co." or "Scovill Mfg Co." aid dating and authenticity checks[2][9].  

### Authentication Techniques  
- **Material Analysis:** Pewter buttons oxidize to a dull gray, while brass develops a patina. Modern reproductions often use lighter alloys[2][9].  
- **Shank Inspection:** Hand-soldered shanks indicate pre-20th century origin; machine-pressed shanks suggest modern replicas[4][9].  
- **Design Consistency:** Misaligned eagles or anachronistic motifs (e.g., post-1902 Great Seal on a "Civil War" button) signal fakes[13][14].

## Acquisition and Preservation Strategies  

### Sourcing Authentic Buttons  
- **Estate Sales:** Unmarked collections occasionally yield pre-1900 buttons priced below market value[5][12].  
- **Metal Detecting Clubs:** Partnerships with groups like the **Federation of Metal Detectorists** can access dug specimens, though conservation is critical[2][10].  
- **Military Surplus Stores:** Post-WWII buttons are often undervalued here, with **Navy gilt buttons** selling for $8–$15[7].  

### Preservation Best Practices  
- **Storage:** Acid-free trays lined with inert polyester foam prevent oxidation; avoid PVC-containing plastics[8][12].  
- **Cleaning:** Use soft brushes for dirt removal; submerging in distilled water (5–10 minutes) loosens grime without damaging patinas[8].  
- **Display:** UV-filtering glass cases mitigate discoloration; humidity controls (40–50% RH) deter metal fatigue[8][12].
