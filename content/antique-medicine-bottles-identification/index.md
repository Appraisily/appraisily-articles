Antique medicine bottle identification involves analyzing historical glass containers used for pharmaceuticals to determine their age, origin, and significance. This practice preserves medical history while enabling collectors to assess value and authenticity. Below is a structured guide to this specialized field.

## 1. Fundamentals of Antique Medicine Bottle Identification  
Identification focuses on analyzing physical characteristics like **glass color**, **embossing patterns**, **manufacturing marks**, and **shape profiles**. Key elements include:  
- **Pontil marks**: Rough scars on bottle bases from hand-blown production (pre-1860s) [10][14]  
- **Mold seams**: Vertical lines indicating machine-made bottles (post-1900) [13][14]  
- **Embossed text**: Pharmacy names, locations, or product claims (e.g., "cure" pre-1912 Sherley Amendment) [1][8]  

This process is vital for preserving pharmaceutical history, tracing regional medical practices, and detecting counterfeit items in collections.

## 2. Historical Evolution  
- **18th-19th century**: Hand-blown bottles with pontil marks; dark amber/green glass to protect light-sensitive contents [2][8]  
- **Late 1800s**: Rise of embossed branding for patent medicines like Hostetter's Bitters [1][8]  
- **1910s-1930s**: Transition to machine-made bottles with uniform seams; plastic components introduced post-1940 [1][5]  
- **Post-1970**: DEA regulations requiring permits to sell bottles containing controlled substances [3][7]

## 4. Valuation Factors  
- **Rarity**: Pre-1900 embossed pharmacy bottles ($50-$300) vs. common 1940s aspirin bottles ($5-$15) [6][8]  
- **Condition**: Intact labels add 200-400% value (e.g., $30 Leedom's Drug Store bottle vs. $5 unlabeled) [1][6]  
- **Historical ties**: Prohibition-era bitters bottles with alcohol content documentation [1][3]  
- **Aesthetics**: Cobalt blue bottles command 50% premiums over clear glass [4][8]  

## 5. Authentication Techniques  
1. **Base inspection**: Seek pontil marks or mold numbers (e.g., "7" for Whitall Tatum Co.) [10][11]  
2. **UV light test**: Detect modern glass additives absent in pre-1920s bottles [9][14]  
3. **Seam analysis**: Partial seams indicate pre-1900 hand-tooled finishes [13][14]  
4. **Label cross-referencing**: Match embossed names to historical directories like Fike's *The Bottle Book* [8]
