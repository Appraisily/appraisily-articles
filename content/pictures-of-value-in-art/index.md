## 1. Defining Value in Art and Its Importance  

### Technical Value: Light, Shadow, and Perception  
In art theory, "value" refers to the lightness or darkness of tones, which artists manipulate to create three-dimensional illusions on flat surfaces. A gray shape becomes a cube through strategic shading[1], while landscape painters like Claude Monet use atmospheric perspective—lighter values for distant elements—to simulate depth[1][2]. This technique, rooted in Renaissance practices, remains foundational for realism. For instance, Caravaggio’s *The Calling of Saint Matthew* (1599–1600) uses extreme contrasts (*tenebrism*) to dramatize figures, a method later adopted by filmmakers like Ridley Scott[2][9].  

### Cultural and Economic Value  
Beyond technique, value denotes an artwork’s monetary or symbolic worth. Gustav Klimt’s *Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer I* (1907) exemplifies this duality: its gold-leaf aesthetics reflect Vienna’s Art Nouveau movement, while its $135 million sale in 2006 underscores its market prestige[5][11].
