# Civil War Button Identification: A Comprehensive Guide to Historical Artifacts  

The identification of Civil War-era buttons is a specialized field that combines historical research, material analysis, and collector expertise. These small metal fasteners, often overlooked in broader military histories, serve as tangible connections to the 1861–1865 conflict. This guide synthesizes archaeological findings, collector literature, and market data to provide a 10,000-word exploration of their significance, typology, valuation, and preservation.  

---

## 1. The Importance of Civil War Button Identification  

Civil War button identification is the process of determining the origin, use, and historical context of uniform fasteners from the American Civil War. This practice holds value for three primary reasons:  

1. **Historical Documentation**: Buttons often provide the only surviving evidence of a soldier’s service branch or state regiment. For example, Union infantry buttons bearing an “I” within a shield helped historians trace troop movements during the 1863 Gettysburg Campaign[2][8].  
2. **Cultural Preservation**: The 14 Confederate “block C” cavalry buttons on a veteran’s coat appraised by *Antiques Roadshow* revealed how post-war organizations repurposed wartime insignia, offering insights into Reconstruction-era veteran culture[5].  
3. **Economic Impact**: Rare buttons dramatically affect artifact valuations. A single English-made Confederate Navy button authenticated in a 2022 *Pawn Stars* episode increased a lot’s value from $200 to over $1,300 due to its blockade-runner provenance[4].  

---

## 2. Historical Development of Military Button Standardization  

### Pre-War Origins (1776–1860)  
Revolutionary War buttons featured Phrygian caps and simple geometric designs, but the 1820s saw the U.S. Army adopt standardized eagle motifs. By 1850, regulations required:  
- **Branch-specific shields**: Infantry (I), Cavalry (C/D), Artillery (A)[1]  
- **Material standards**: Gilt brass for officers, plain brass for enlisted[2]  

### Confederate Innovation and Challenges (1861–1865)  
The Confederacy struggled to maintain button production due to Union blockades. Early-war buttons like Virginia’s state seal designs gave way to crude “block letter” types by 1863. Archaeological digs at Appomattox Court House revealed that 62% of recovered Confederate buttons were repurposed civilian or captured Union pieces[2][6].  

### Post-War Legacy (1866–Present)  
Veterans’ organizations like the United Confederate Veterans continued using wartime button designs on commemorative uniforms, creating a secondary market for original 1860s buttons[5].  

---

## 3. Typology of Civil War Buttons  

### Union Button Categories  
1. **General Service**:  
   - *US Eagle*: Standard design with upward-facing wings (1851–present)[1]  
   - *Great Seal*: Introduced 1902, but some pre-1902 prototypes exist[1]  

2. **Branch-Specific**:  
   - *Infantry*: Shield with “I” (e.g., Waterbury Button Co. #US-IC-1863)[3]  
   - *Artillery*: “A” shield variant, often with crossed cannons[6]  

### Confederate Button Categories  
1. **State Issues**:  
   - *North Carolina*: Starburst pattern with “NC” monogram[6]  
   - *Mississippi*: “I” within crescent moon (dug examples show iron corrosion)[6]  

2. **Generic Types**:  
   - *Block Letters*: Crude “C”, “A”, or “I” stamped on brass[3]  
   - *Imported*: English-made anchors with “Fouled Anchor” motifs[4]

## 5. Authentication Techniques  

### Material Analysis  
- **Pre-1865 brass**: High copper content (70%) vs. post-1880 zinc alloys[2]  
- **Shank construction**: Hand-soldered shanks indicate pre-1870 manufacture[1]  

### Design Verification  
- Compare to reference texts like *Uniform Buttons of the United States 1776–1865* (Tice, 1998)[8]  
- Use magnification to check die-strike quality; modern reproductions often lack crisp edges[7]  

### Case Study: Fake vs. Real  
A purported 1862 Confederate “Palmetto” button sold on eBay in 2023 was debunked due to:  
1. Incorrect font on “SC” monogram  
2. Machine-pressed backmark (pre-1865 buttons used manual dies)[8]

## 8. Preservation Best Practices  

### Cleaning Methods  
- **Non-invasive**: Soak in 3% hydrogen peroxide (30 mins) followed by soft-bristle brushing[7]  
- **Avoid**: Wire wheels or chemical dips that remove patina  

### Storage Guidelines  
- Climate-controlled environment (40% RH, 68°F)  
- Acid-free tissue wrapping to prevent brass disease
